Nearly half of all college students are rethinking their majors because of AI.

That number comes from a [new Gallup and Lumina Foundation study](https://news.gallup.com/poll/704087/college-students-weigh-impact-majors-careers.aspx) released this week. Forty-seven percent of students have given at least "a fair amount" of thought to switching fields due to AI's potential impact on the job market. Sixteen percent have already made the switch. Among associate degree students, the number is even higher: 56% are questioning what they signed up for.

Read those numbers carefully. These are not panicked freshmen catastrophizing after a TikTok. These are students doing a rational cost-benefit analysis on their own futures. And by at least one measure, they are reading the room more clearly than most corporate strategists.

Here is what they are seeing.

## The Layoffs Are Real

[Business Insider's latest tally](https://www.businessinsider.com/list-companies-replacing-human-employees-with-ai-layoffs-workforce-reductions) documents company after company citing AI efficiencies as a rationale for workforce reductions in 2026. Block eliminated approximately 40% of its staff. Klarna's headcount has halved over four years, and its CEO has said it will shrink further. IBM, Salesforce, and Atlassian are all on the list. A March report from Challenger, Gray, and Christmas found that AI has already been cited in 8% of this year's job cut announcements. That number will go up.

And it is not just blue-collar work. [Stanford research tracking early-career workers](https://www.insidehighered.com/news/quick-takes/2026/04/02/ai-pushing-students-consider-changing-majors-data-shows) in AI-exposed occupations like software development and clerical work found a 16% relative employment decline between 2022 and 2025. Entry-level white-collar workers are bearing the brunt. More experienced workers, doing work that requires deeper judgment and relationships, have remained largely stable.

That gap is not a coincidence. It is the signal.

## The Agentic Moment Has Arrived

Meanwhile, BNY, the world's largest custody bank, has [deployed 20,000 AI assistants across its global workforce](https://business.times-online.com/times-online/article/tokenring-2026-1-16-bny-mellon-scales-the-agentic-era-with-deployment-of-20000-ai-assistants). The company is calling this the "agentic era." Every employee now has an AI collaborator. The institution's explicit goal is to build what it calls AI literacy at scale: not just using AI for simple tasks, but integrating it into judgment-intensive work.

Think about what that means for hiring. The entry-level tasks that once gave young professionals their first foothold. Filing, formatting, summarizing, scheduling, first-draft writing, data pulling. Those tasks are dissolving into agent workflows. BNY is not doing this to shrink its team. It is doing this to raise the floor on what every employee is expected to contribute.

What remains, what BNY is actually trying to scale, is the human layer that AI cannot replicate.

## What the 4I Model Predicts

In *After the Grind*, I argue that AI's most important effect is not elimination. It is clarification. By removing the grind, which is the low-judgment, high-volume, routine execution that once filled most workdays, AI forces a reckoning: what is the distinctly human contribution left?

The answer organizes around four skills. Interpretation: making sense of data and ambiguity in ways that require context, judgment, and stakes. Integration: connecting information across domains, bridging the gap between what the machine produces and what an organization actually needs to do. Interpersonal connection: the trust, persuasion, empathy, and relationship-building that determines whether a good idea ever becomes action. And imagination: the ability to ask questions the model was never trained to ask.

None of those four things appear in a standard first-year business curriculum. Which is exactly the problem the Gallup data is surfacing.

## Higher Education's Uncomfortable Moment

[A Deloitte analysis of 2026 higher education trends](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/insights/industry/articles-on-higher-education/2026-higher-education-trends.html) notes that an MIT study found up to 12% of the U.S. workforce could be replaced by AI tools. Deloitte's recommendation to colleges: review programs more frequently, foster real-time communication between employers and faculty, and expand internships and apprenticeship programs. Seventy-three percent of graduates who completed a paid internship landed degree-required jobs, compared to just 44% without one.

That last number should stop every department chair in their tracks. The experiential gap, the space between classroom and workplace, is where the 4I skills either get developed or don't. And right now, most universities are still designing curricula around the grind that AI is rapidly eliminating.

The students panicking about their majors are not wrong to be concerned. They are, in fact, asking the right question. They are asking whether their degree will prepare them for the work that actually remains. In too many programs, the honest answer is: not yet.

## The Right Response Is Not Fear. It Is Reorientation.

Here is the thing about the 47% who are rethinking their paths. They are not necessarily in the wrong major. A marketing degree, a finance degree, an operations degree: none of those are obsolete. The technical content remains useful. What has to change is what surrounds it.

If your program does not teach students to interpret ambiguous data without a rubric, it is preparing them for work AI already does. If it does not put them in rooms where they must persuade, negotiate, and build trust under pressure, they will arrive at their first job without the skills that will matter most. If it does not train imagination, meaning the capacity to reframe problems and generate options that were not on the menu, they will be excellent at prompting AI and not much else.

The students changing their majors are not running from AI. They are running toward something safer. They deserve programs that can tell them clearly: here is what safe looks like in 2030, and here is how we are preparing you for it.

That clarity does not come from swapping a statistics class for a prompt engineering class. It comes from rebuilding around what the machine cannot replace. Interpretation, integration, interpersonal connection, imagination.

Start there.
