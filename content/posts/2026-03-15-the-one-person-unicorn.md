At Morgan Stanley's annual TMT Conference in San Francisco last week, the most common question from investors wasn't about GPU supply chains, cloud revenue, or which AI model is winning the benchmark wars. According to analyst Adam Jonas, it was simpler and more unsettling: ["What will our kids do?"](https://fortune.com/2026/03/12/ai-jobs-future-morgan-stanley-tmt-conference-ceos/)

That question hung in the air alongside some staggering numbers. OpenAI CEO Sam Altman told conference attendees he can envision one person, maybe five, running an entire billion-dollar company. Venture capitalists are already placing bets on who will build the first "one-person unicorn." The timeline? Not a decade out. The next few years.

Meanwhile, the numbers on the other side of the ledger keep climbing. [Over 45,000 tech workers have been laid off in the first months of 2026](https://technode.global/2026/03/09/2026-tech-layoffs-reach-45000-in-march-more-than-9200-due-to-ai-and-automation-rationalfx/), with more than 9,200 of those cuts explicitly tied to AI and automation. Block, Amazon, Meta, Oracle. The list reads like a who's who of companies that were supposed to be the safe bets.

Then came Atlassian. On Wednesday, the collaboration software giant [announced it was cutting roughly 1,600 employees, about 10% of its workforce](https://www.atlassian.com/blog/announcements/atlassian-team-update-march-2026). CEO Mike Cannon-Brookes was more candid than most: "It would be disingenuous to pretend AI doesn't change the mix of skills we need or the number of roles required in certain areas." The company framed the layoffs as "adaptation," not replacement. But the people clearing out their desks probably don't find much comfort in the semantic distinction.

And here is what makes this moment different from previous waves of tech restructuring. These companies aren't struggling. Atlassian just posted 25%+ cloud revenue growth. Morgan Stanley's analysts noted that high-income consumers, whose portfolios are swelling with AI-driven gains, are spending more. The cuts aren't about survival. They're about optimization. Companies are discovering they can do the same work, or more, with fewer people. The grind, the repetitive cognitive labor that justified large teams, is being absorbed by machines.

**The Grind Is Disappearing. What Replaces It?**

This is the core argument I make in *After the Grind*. For decades, we built careers around our ability to process, organize, and execute routine cognitive tasks. Research. Data entry. Report generation. Scheduling. Email management. Code that follows established patterns. These were the bread and butter of white-collar employment. They were THE GRIND.

AI doesn't just assist with this work. It eliminates the need for humans to do it at all. And when you remove THE GRIND from the equation, you have to ask: what's left? What do humans still do that justifies a seat at the table?

The answer is what I call the 4I model: Interpretation, Integration, Interpersonal connection, and Imagination.

**Interpretation** is the ability to look at data, a situation, or a set of options and make a judgment call that accounts for context, nuance, and values. AI can generate a market analysis. It takes a human to decide what it means for *your* company, *your* customers, *your* moment.

**Integration** is the capacity to synthesize knowledge across domains. The most valuable professionals aren't the ones who know one thing deeply. They're the ones who can connect marketing insight to supply chain reality to regulatory constraint to customer psychology. AI is excellent within silos. Humans bridge them.

**Interpersonal connection** is the skill that Atlassian's layoff memo inadvertently highlighted. When Cannon-Brookes wrote that "people and AI create the best outcomes," he was pointing at something real. Trust, negotiation, mentorship, the ability to read a room, to inspire a team through uncertainty: these are not automatable because they depend on genuine human presence. You can't outsource vulnerability to a language model.

**Imagination** is the wildcard. Not creativity in the "generate 50 logo options" sense. AI does that fine. Imagination is the ability to envision something that doesn't exist yet and marshal resources to build it. It's the entrepreneurial instinct. The strategic leap. The question nobody thought to ask.

**The Education Question**

Which brings us back to "What will our kids do?" and directly into the classroom.

The Guardian [reported this week](https://www.theguardian.com/technology/ng-interactive/2026/mar/10/ai-impact-professors-students-learning) on professors across the country scrambling to save critical thinking in the age of AI. One professor's quote captured the mood perfectly: "I wish I could push ChatGPT off a cliff." The concern isn't just about cheating. It's about a generation of students who may never develop the interpretive and integrative muscles that THE GRIND used to build as a side effect.

Here's the paradox. THE GRIND was inefficient, but it was also formative. Writing a terrible first draft taught you how to think through an argument. Crunching numbers by hand gave you intuition for when results looked wrong. The repetitive work built cognitive scaffolding that supported higher-order thinking.

If AI removes that scaffolding before students have a chance to build it, we have a real problem. Not because the tasks themselves mattered, but because the *learning* embedded in them did.

The Washington Post [published an op-ed last week](https://www.washingtonpost.com/opinions/2026/03/02/ais-best-use-is-enhancing-human-judgment-so-study-liberal-arts/) arguing that higher education is learning exactly the wrong lesson from AI's rise. Universities are racing to launch AI programs, data science certificates, prompt engineering courses. But as the author argues, "Artificial intelligence makes purely technical skills less valuable and human judgment more essential." The institutions that will serve students best aren't the ones teaching them to build AI. They're the ones teaching them to think alongside it.

This is not a popular opinion in a higher education landscape that measures value in starting salaries and job placement rates. But it's right. When the technical floor rises (and it is rising fast), the differentiator becomes the human ceiling. Philosophy. Ethics. Communication. The ability to frame a problem before solving it.

**The Fork in the Road**

We are at a fork, and the path we choose in the next few years will echo for decades. One road leads to what one professor in the Guardian piece called "a degraded, soulless form of vocational training administered by AI instructors." The other leads to an education system that takes the 4I model seriously: that trains students not to compete with AI on speed and volume, but to do the things AI structurally cannot.

Sam Altman might be right that one person will someday run a billion-dollar company. But that person won't succeed because they're good at prompting AI models. They'll succeed because they can interpret what the models miss, integrate across domains the models can't bridge, connect with the humans the models can't reach, and imagine futures the models can't dream.

The question isn't whether AI will eliminate THE GRIND. It already is. The question is what we're building in its place. And right now, based on this week's headlines, we're still figuring it out.

That's not necessarily bad news. It just means the window for shaping the answer is still open. But it won't be open forever.
