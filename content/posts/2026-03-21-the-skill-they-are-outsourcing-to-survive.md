March 2026 is turning out to be a clarifying month. Not comfortable, but clarifying.

[Forty-five thousand tech workers lost their jobs this month](https://opentools.ai/news/2026-tech-layoffs-hit-45000-in-march-ai-and-automation-take-the-lead), and the headline number is striking enough. But the detail buried inside is what matters: more than 9,200 of those cuts were directly attributed to AI and automation. Amazon, Google, and Microsoft are not making these moves because business is bad. They are making them because machines now do the grind better than people do, and these companies have done the math.

At roughly the same time, the IMF's managing director warned that AI is [hitting the labor market "like a tsunami."](https://www.economist.com/finance-and-economics/2026/01/26/why-ai-wont-wipe-out-white-collar-jobs) Dario Amodei, the CEO of Anthropic, went further: he predicted that AI could eliminate [half of all entry-level white-collar jobs](https://www.economist.com/finance-and-economics/2026/01/26/why-ai-wont-wipe-out-white-collar-jobs) in the near term. Half. The jobs where people used to prove themselves before earning more responsibility. Gone.

And then there is a story from inside a Stanford classroom that I cannot stop thinking about.

A literature professor named Lea Pao [asked her students to visit a local museum, spend ten minutes with a painting, and write a few paragraphs about the experience](https://www.theguardian.com/technology/ng-interactive/2026/mar/10/ai-impact-professors-students-learning). Deliberately personal. Deliberately human. One student, unable to get into the museum on a Monday when it was closed, simply asked an AI to write the reflection instead. The result, Professor Pao said, was "too perfect, without saying anything."

That phrase deserves to sit with you for a moment. Too perfect, without saying anything.

---

Here is what connects these three stories, and why they matter together rather than separately.

The layoffs are not random. They are targeted. What is being eliminated is processing work: tasks that require moving information from one place to another, applying consistent rules to standard inputs, producing predictable outputs at scale. This is precisely the work that most entry-level roles have always been built around. You proved your reliability by doing it well. You earned your way to the more interesting problems.

That ladder is collapsing. The rungs at the bottom are being sawed off.

Simultaneously, inside the institutions that are supposed to prepare people for this economy, students are doing something perfectly rational and catastrophically short-sighted. They are using AI to skip the hard parts of learning. The museum reflection, the research paper, the analytical memo, the case write-up: all of it is increasingly being outsourced to tools that are, as Professor Pao put it, too perfect, without saying anything.

Here is the irony that should disturb every business school faculty member reading this. The skills students are bypassing with AI are exactly the skills that the post-grind economy will pay for. The skills machines cannot replicate. The skills that the workforce now desperately needs.

---

In my book *After the Grind*, I argue that as AI absorbs the processing layer of knowledge work, human value migrates upward to four things I call the 4I model: interpretation, integration, interpersonal connection, and imagination.

**Interpretation** is the ability to look at messy, ambiguous, incomplete information and construct a coherent meaning from it. Not a perfect summary. A meaning. A judgment call backed by experience and context.

**Integration** is the ability to connect ideas across domains, to see the link between a supply chain disruption and a marketing strategy, between behavioral psychology and a pricing decision.

**Interpersonal connection** is the capacity to build trust with another human, to read a room, to know when the data says one thing and the client needs something else entirely.

**Imagination** is the ability to envision something that does not yet exist and care enough to try to build it.

None of these are skills you develop by having an AI write your museum reflection. None of them are skills you exercise when you let a chatbot draft your analysis before you have even formed an opinion. You build these skills the old-fashioned way: by doing the hard thinking, tolerating the discomfort of not knowing, arriving at something original, and discovering through the process what you actually think.

The student who used AI to write that reflection did not just hand in a dishonest assignment. She deprived herself of a moment of genuine skill development in the skills that will define her career.

---

I want to be fair here. The pressure students are under is real. They are navigating a world that has changed faster than their education has. They have watched hiring slow down, heard the predictions about entry-level jobs, and arrived at a reasonable if unfortunate conclusion: use every tool available to produce work that looks good fast. That is a rational response to a set of incentives that higher education has not yet recalibrated.

[An Education Week report this month](https://www.edweek.org/teaching-learning/bold-changes-needed-to-prepare-students-for-ai-fueled-disruption-commission-says/2026/03) called for "bold changes" to prepare students for AI-fueled disruption. The Guardian piece on professors and AI noted that a widening divide is emerging: elite institutions moving toward more personal, high-touch, technology-limited experiences, while mass higher education increasingly leans into AI integration without asking hard questions about what gets lost.

That divide has a name. It is the divide between institutions that are preparing people for the post-grind economy and institutions that are still preparing people for the grind itself, just with better tools.

---

The March layoff numbers are not a warning. We are past warnings. They are a data point in a trend that has been building for three years and is now accelerating. The companies doing the cutting are not in crisis. They are profitable, confident, and making deliberate strategic choices about which human capabilities are worth paying for.

The answer they keep arriving at is not the answer that looks good on a processing-heavy resume. It is the answer that looks like a person who can walk into a museum, stand in front of a painting, and say something true about it that a machine cannot say.

The students using AI to fake that skill are not cheating the professor. They are cheating themselves out of the one thing the economy will actually pay for.

That is the clarification March 2026 is offering. Whether the institutions training them take it seriously is the open question.
