The most telling moment at [Morgan Stanley's annual TMT Conference](https://fortune.com/2026/03/12/ai-jobs-future-morgan-stanley-tmt-conference-ceos/) this week didn't come from a product launch or an earnings beat. It came from a question. Analyst Adam Jonas told attendees that the single most common thing investors asked him, across panels and fireside chats with the most powerful people in tech, was this:

"What will our kids do?"

That question deserves to sit with you for a second. These are not worried parents at a school board meeting. These are the people funding the AI revolution, the ones whose portfolios are swelling with unprecedented gains. And even they are looking at their own children and wondering what's left.

The headlines this week suggest they're right to wonder. But the answer, I think, is more interesting than the anxiety.

## The Numbers Keep Coming

[Atlassian slashed 1,600 jobs on Wednesday](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/03/11/atlassian-slashes-10percent-of-workforce-to-self-fund-investments-in-ai.html), a clean 10% of its workforce, to "self-fund" its pivot toward AI and enterprise sales. This follows [Vendasta quietly eliminating 70 roles](https://www.prismnews.com/topics/agency-seo-growth/vendasta-cuts-workforce-as-ai-automation-takes-over-key) as AI automation replaced key business processes. And according to [The Financial Express](https://www.financialexpress.com/life/technology-ai-layoffs-2026-from-meta-oracle-to-amazon-tech-companies-cut-over-35000-jobs-worldwide-amid-ai-restructuring-4166698/), major tech companies including Meta, Oracle, and Amazon have collectively cut more than 35,000 jobs worldwide so far in 2026.

Let that number breathe. Thirty-five thousand. And we're only in March.

Sam Altman told the Morgan Stanley crowd he can now envision one to five people running an entire company, and that the timeline has compressed to "the next few years." Jensen Huang summarized the moment in three words: "Compute equals revenue." The message from the top is unambiguous. The grind, the repetitive cognitive labor that has defined white-collar careers for decades, is being automated at a pace that even the people building these systems didn't expect.

## But Here's What the Panic Misses

While the layoff numbers dominate headlines, a [new report from Anthropic](https://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/relationships/6-hands-on-jobs-surprisingly-safe-from-ai-according-to-anthropics-report/photostory/129454608.cms) released this month quietly reframes the entire conversation. Their researchers introduced a metric called "Observed Exposure," which measures not what AI *could* theoretically do, but what people are *actually using it for* in their daily work.

The finding? About 30% of the workforce sits in a "Zero Exposure" zone. Cooks, mechanics, lifeguards, bartenders. Jobs that require navigating the messy, unpredictable, physical real world. As the report puts it: AI can write a sonnet about a broken engine, but it can't pick up a wrench to fix it.

And here's the inversion that matters: the most exposed workers aren't the ones we traditionally thought of as vulnerable to automation. Computer programmers face a 75% exposure rate. Financial analysts are close behind. Graduate degree holders are nearly four times more likely to be in high-exposure roles than those without degrees. The old narrative that automation replaces blue-collar work while white-collar professionals stay safe? It's been completely flipped.

This is exactly the shift I describe in *After the Grind*. The grind was never really about physical labor. It was about the repetitive cognitive tasks that filled knowledge work: processing data, formatting reports, writing boilerplate code, drafting templated communications. That's the work AI is devouring. The jobs that survive and thrive are the ones that require what I call the 4I skills: Interpretation, Integration, Interpersonal connection, and Imagination.

## The University Question

Which brings us to higher education, where the tension is sharpest. A [major new survey from HEPI](https://www.hepi.ac.uk/reports/student-generative-ai-survey-2026/) (the Higher Education Policy Institute) found that AI use is now "near universal" among UK undergraduates. Ninety-five percent of students use AI in at least one way. Ninety-four percent use it to help with assessed work. The proportion directly including AI-generated text in submissions has risen from 3% in 2024 to 12% in 2026.

But here's the statistic that should keep every department chair up at night: while 68% of students believe AI skills are essential to thrive in today's world, fewer than half feel their teaching staff are helping them develop those skills. Only 36% feel encouraged by their institution to use AI at all.

Universities are in a bind. The very skills we've been assessing, the ability to produce competent analytical writing, to process and summarize information, to solve well-defined problems, are precisely the skills AI handles most capably. If your curriculum is built around grind-based assessments, you're training students for jobs that are disappearing while they're still enrolled.

The answer isn't to ban AI or to pretend it doesn't exist. The answer is to redesign education around the 4I skills that AI can't replicate.

**Interpretation**: Can you look at the same data everyone else has access to and see something different? Can you identify what matters, not just what's measurable?

**Integration**: Can you connect insights across domains, disciplines, and stakeholder perspectives in ways that create new value?

**Interpersonal connection**: Can you build trust, navigate conflict, read a room, lead a team through ambiguity? The HEPI survey found that 15% of students are already using AI for companionship and to address loneliness. That's not a sign AI is replacing human connection. It's a sign human connection is undersupplied.

**Imagination**: Can you envision something that doesn't exist yet? Can you ask the question nobody thought to ask?

## The Real Answer

So what will our kids do? The honest answer: not what we did. Not the spreadsheet work, not the data processing, not the report formatting, not the code that writes itself now. Those careers aren't disappearing next decade. They're disappearing next year.

But the Morgan Stanley investors asking that question are revealing something important about themselves. They're asking it because they know, intuitively, that the value their own children will need to create can't be reduced to a prompt. The future belongs to people who can interpret complexity, integrate across boundaries, connect with other humans in ways that build real trust, and imagine possibilities that no model would generate on its own.

The grind is ending. That's not the crisis. The crisis is that we've spent decades telling young people the grind *is* the career. Now we have to help them see what's on the other side.

The kids will be fine. But only if we stop preparing them for the world that's leaving and start preparing them for the one that's arriving.
