The numbers keep getting bigger.

This week, professional services firm Cognizant [updated its forecast](https://fortune.com/2026/03/19/ai-jobs-vulnerable-disruption-layoffs-warning-price-tag/) on AI's impact on the labor market, and the revision was striking. Back in 2023, they analyzed 18,000 tasks and nearly 1,000 job categories using Department of Labor data. Now they've rerun the numbers. The result: 93% of jobs face at least some disruption from AI. Thirty percent face an existential threat. That's 15 percentage points higher than their original estimate. In total, they put the value of labor AI could absorb at $4.5 trillion.

"We underestimated the impact of the technology," the report reads. "What we projected might take until 2032 to unfold is happening now before our eyes."

Meanwhile, [HSBC is reportedly weighing cuts](https://www.reuters.com/business/world-at-work/hsbc-mulls-job-cuts-that-could-impact-around-20000-roles-bloomberg-news-reports-2026-03-19/) that could affect around 20,000 roles, roughly 10% of its global workforce. The bank is specifically targeting non-client-facing roles in global service centers: exactly the kind of back-office processing work that AI handles without complaint, at scale, around the clock. Goldman Sachs economists, for their part, estimated earlier this year that AI was responsible for [5,000 to 10,000 net job losses per month](https://www.reuters.com/business/world-at-work/companies-cutting-jobs-investments-shift-toward-ai-2026-03-19/) in the most exposed U.S. industries last year.

And over in HR leadership circles? A new [CHRO Survey from the CHRO Association](https://www.morningstar.com/news/pr-newswire/20260320ph15371/2026-survey-reveals-ai-dominates-focus-for-hr-executives-as-uncertainty-abounds), conducted in partnership with the University of South Carolina's Darla Moore School of Business, found that 91% of chief human resources officers now rank AI and workplace digitization as their top concern. Not talent acquisition. Not employee engagement. Not DEI. AI. By a wide margin.

These aren't fringe predictions anymore. They're the main event.

So here's the question I keep coming back to, the one I hear from my students and from professionals who've read my book: if 93% of jobs are vulnerable, what exactly are you supposed to do with that?

I want to offer something more useful than "learn to code" or "focus on soft skills." Let me try.

---

**The 93% figure is not a death sentence. It's a description.**

When Cognizant says 93% of jobs face disruption, they don't mean 93% of jobs will disappear. Disruption is not elimination. A job that gets disrupted is a job where certain tasks get automated, certain workflows get restructured, certain roles evolve. That process has been happening since the steam engine. What makes this wave different is its speed and its white-collar reach.

The work being automated, almost universally, is what I call the Grind. Repetitive processing. Data entry and retrieval. Routine report generation. Compliance checklists. Template-based communications. First drafts that follow a predictable structure. The Grind is exactly what it sounds like: work that has to get done, work that is often done correctly by diligent people, but work that does not require a human mind at its most powerful.

This is actually good news if you reframe it correctly.

The Grind was occupying enormous amounts of human capacity that could have been used for something else. Now it can be. The question is whether individuals and organizations will use that freed capacity wisely.

---

**The four things that don't automate**

In my book, I argue that human value in an AI-augmented economy concentrates around four capabilities. I call them the 4I model: Interpretation, Integration, Interpersonal connection, and Imagination.

Interpretation is the ability to make sense of information in context. AI can analyze data but it cannot tell you what the data means for your specific organization, your specific customers, your specific moment in history. That judgment requires lived experience, domain knowledge, and accountability. Machines are not accountable.

Integration is the ability to synthesize across disciplines. The most valuable professionals today are not deep specialists who know one thing thoroughly. They are people who can connect a marketing insight to an operations problem, or link a regulatory change to a customer experience design choice. AI can retrieve information from multiple domains but cannot build the relational reasoning that comes from years of cross-functional experience.

Interpersonal connection is the human capacity for trust, persuasion, empathy, and relationship. HSBC can automate 20,000 back-office roles. They cannot automate the relationship manager who calls a long-tenured client the day after a market downturn, simply to listen. The human moment in high-stakes interactions has economic value precisely because it is irreplaceable.

Imagination is the ability to conceive of something that does not yet exist. New products. New business models. New metaphors that help people understand a complicated idea. AI is a powerful creative tool, but it works by recombining existing patterns. True novelty still starts with a human asking "what if?"

The jobs being cut at HSBC, the roles Goldman is counting in its monthly job-loss tally: they are, overwhelmingly, Grind jobs. Processing roles. Verification roles. Templated communication roles. These are not jobs built around Interpretation, Integration, Interpersonal connection, or Imagination. They are jobs built around execution of defined tasks at scale. And that is precisely what AI does best.

---

**What universities need to understand right now**

This is where higher education enters the picture. The [AI in Higher Education Summit](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=6441542), held in Paris just last week at ESCP Business School, brought together researchers and practitioners to wrestle with exactly this challenge. And [universities are moving](https://blog.bccresearch.com/universities-are-going-all-in-on-ai): BCC Research's AI Sentiment Index for higher education signals that institutions are no longer experimenting with AI, they are institutionalizing it.

But institutionalizing AI tools is not the same as building the right curriculum. You can give every student access to the best AI platforms available and still graduate them unprepared if the underlying coursework still rewards Grind-type thinking: memorization, procedural problem-solving, templated analysis, rule-following. The 91% of CHROs who are obsessed with AI are not looking for graduates who can use the software. They are looking for graduates who can do the things the software cannot.

That is the realignment higher education needs to make: from training students to execute tasks correctly, to training students to think originally, judge wisely, connect genuinely, and build imaginatively. Not as a replacement for rigor, but as the new form of it.

---

When a Fortune 500 firm revises its forecast from "this will take until 2032" to "this is happening now," that is a signal worth taking seriously. The $4.5 trillion is not a scare number. It is a description of a massive transfer of economic energy. The Grind is being handed off.

The question for every professional, every student, and every university is the same: what are you doing with the capacity that transfer frees up?

That is the only career question that matters right now.
