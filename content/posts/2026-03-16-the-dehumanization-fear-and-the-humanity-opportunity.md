Something interesting happened in the news cycle today. Two stories landed within hours of each other that, taken together, tell you almost everything you need to know about where work is headed.

First: [Reuters reported](https://www.reuters.com/business/world-at-work/meta-planning-sweeping-layoffs-ai-costs-mount-2026-03-14/) that Meta is planning to cut up to 20% of its workforce as AI costs mount and automation takes over functions that used to require entire teams. This follows a pattern we've been watching all year. Block, Atlassian, Amazon. The list keeps growing. [By one count](https://beincrypto.com/ai-agents-job-loss-2026/), AI agents have now been directly linked to over 9,200 job losses in 2026 alone, and we're barely through Q1.

Second: a [new national survey from Resume Now](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/63-of-workers-say-ai-will-make-the-workplace-feel-less-human-in-2026-302713735.html) found that 63% of American workers believe AI will make the workplace feel less human this year. Not less productive. Not less efficient. Less *human*. And when asked to name their biggest concern, the top answer wasn't job loss (though that came in at 29%). It was skill erosion: 57% of respondents said AI reducing human skills would be the biggest workforce issue of the year.

Read those two data points side by side. Companies are cutting headcount because AI makes certain work unnecessary. Workers are worried not just about losing jobs, but about losing the skills that make them feel competent and valuable. The layoffs are about efficiency. The anxiety is about identity.

This is the territory I've been writing about in *After the Grind*, and it's becoming less theoretical by the week.

## The Real Fear Isn't Unemployment

Over at The Guardian, Gene Marks wrote a [fascinating piece](https://www.theguardian.com/business/2026/mar/15/ai-future-of-work) imagining what happens if the optimists are right. What if AI really does eliminate most routine work? What if some form of universal income makes traditional employment optional? His conclusion was surprisingly honest: even in that best case scenario, we have a massive problem. People don't just work for money. They work for meaning, structure, and social connection.

The Resume Now data backs this up precisely. Workers aren't primarily afraid of being broke. They're afraid of being irrelevant. Of waking up one day and realizing the skills they spent a decade building can now be replicated by a $20/month subscription. That's not an economic fear. That's an existential one.

Meanwhile, the [Washington Post published an interactive analysis](https://www.washingtonpost.com/technology/interactive/2026/jobs-most-affected-ai-automation/) of which workers are most vulnerable to AI displacement. The finding that should concern everyone in higher education: this time around, it's white collar professionals in the crosshairs. Not factory workers. Not truck drivers. The people who did everything right. Got the degree, climbed the ladder, mastered the spreadsheet. Those are the jobs that AI handles most easily because they were already digital, already structured, already routine.

A [Dallas Fed report](https://www.telegraphherald.com/news/business/article_28dfb9a6-0f41-57c1-9bc6-141155d23db0.html) added another layer: AI is making it harder for young people to land entry-level positions at all. The traditional on-ramp to a career, where you do the grunt work, learn the ropes, and gradually take on more responsibility, is being compressed or eliminated entirely. Companies are automating the bottom rungs of the ladder and then wondering why they can't find mid-career talent with the right experience.

## The 4I Framework in Real Time

Here's where the conventional narrative gets it wrong. The story most outlets are telling goes something like this: AI is taking jobs, workers are scared, and someone (government, corporations, educators) needs to fix it. That framing treats human beings as passive recipients of technological disruption. Something happens *to* them. They need to be *retrained*. They require *protection*.

I think that's backwards.

What the Resume Now survey actually reveals, if you look past the headline number, is that workers intuitively understand something that most policymakers haven't caught up to yet. The value of human work was never really about the tasks. It was about the qualities that made those tasks meaningful: judgment, connection, creativity, the ability to see what the data doesn't show.

In the book, I call these the four capabilities that survive automation: Interpretation, Integration, Interpersonal Connection, and Imagination. The 4I model. And every story in today's news cycle points back to them.

**Interpretation**: When the Washington Post maps which jobs are most vulnerable, the common thread isn't industry or salary level. It's the degree to which the work involves making sense of ambiguous, incomplete, or contradictory information. Roles that are primarily about processing clear inputs into standard outputs are the ones that evaporate. The roles that require reading between the lines persist.

**Integration**: Meta isn't just cutting jobs randomly. It's discovering that AI can handle individual functions brilliantly but struggles to connect them into coherent strategy. The 20% reduction will almost certainly be followed by a hiring push for people who can work across silos, synthesize AI outputs from multiple systems, and build something coherent from the pieces. That's integration work. No model does it well yet.

**Interpersonal Connection**: The 63% who say work is becoming less human aren't wrong. They're identifying a real gap. And that gap is also an opportunity. The companies that figure out how to preserve trust, mentorship, and genuine collaboration in an AI-augmented environment will have a massive competitive advantage. That's not a technology problem. That's a leadership problem. A human problem.

**Imagination**: The Guardian piece asks the right question. If AI handles the grind, what do we do with the freedom? The answer depends entirely on whether we've developed the capacity to envision something beyond the next quarterly report. Imagination isn't a soft skill. It's the skill that determines whether AI liberation leads to human flourishing or just a nicer version of boredom.

## What This Means for Students (and Everyone Else)

I teach business students at Washington State University. Many of them are watching these headlines and feeling exactly the anxiety that the Resume Now survey describes. They're asking a reasonable question: why am I learning these skills if a machine can do them better?

The honest answer is that some of what they're learning will indeed become less valuable in the traditional sense. Nobody needs to spend four years learning to build a spreadsheet model when AI does it in seconds. But the ability to look at that model, ask whether it captures reality, spot what's missing, and explain the implications to a room full of skeptical executives? That's worth more now than it was five years ago. Because there's more AI output to interpret, more automated systems to integrate, more people who need a human being to trust.

The fear of dehumanization is valid. Work is becoming less human in the ways that are easiest to automate. The opportunity is that it could become *more* human in the ways that matter most. That shift won't happen automatically. It requires intentional development of the capabilities that make us irreplaceable: not because we can outcompute the machine, but because we can do what it cannot.

Sixty-three percent of workers feel it happening. The question is whether we respond by trying to slow the machine down or by getting serious about building up what makes us human.

I know which bet I'd take.
