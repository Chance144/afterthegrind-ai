A few weeks ago, I wrote about [three essays that each imagined the economic future of AI](https://afterthegrind.ai/posts/2026-02-26-three-futures-one-question/). One predicted collapse. One predicted Bitcoin. One predicted abundance. They agreed on the disruption but disagreed on the ending.

This week, the optimists got some ammunition.

Daniel Jeffries, the same futurist who wrote the abundance scenario I covered in that piece, posted a sharp thread arguing that the AI jobs apocalypse is "the Population Bomb of our time." His evidence: programmer hiring is ticking upward, not downward. The very job most directly affected by AI is seeing more demand, not less.

Rohan Paul made the same argument with more data, pointing to Germany's Bitkom 2025 study: 109,000 unfilled IT positions, with 79% of companies expecting the shortage to worsen. And the key finding: 42% anticipate needing additional IT specialists specifically because of AI adoption.

Their shared explanation is the Jevons Paradox.

## The Paradox That Changes Everything (Maybe)

In 1865, the economist William Stanley Jevons observed something counterintuitive about coal consumption in England. James Watt's steam engine had made coal dramatically more efficient. You would expect efficiency to reduce consumption. Instead, consumption exploded. Cheaper energy per unit meant more total energy used, because suddenly coal-powered machines were economically viable in industries that could never afford them before.

The pattern repeated with every major resource improvement. Cheaper transistors did not mean fewer transistors. We put computers in everything. Cheaper bandwidth did not mean less data consumed. We invented streaming video and TikTok. Cheaper storage did not mean less data stored. We started recording every click, every transaction, every sensor reading.

Now apply this to software development. When AI makes it 10x cheaper to build software, companies do not build the same amount of software with fewer people. They build 10x more software. Features that were never worth the engineering time suddenly become viable. Internal tools that would have taken a team of five now take one person with an AI copilot. Products that would have cost $500,000 to prototype can be tested for $50,000.

The demand for software does not shrink. It explodes. And with it, the demand for the humans who can direct, review, architect, and ship that software.

This is the Jevons argument, and it is historically airtight. Every previous efficiency gain in a resource with elastic demand has produced more consumption, not less.

## Where It Gets Complicated

But here is what Jeffries and Paul are not addressing, and what the CitriniResearch scenario I wrote about last month traces in painful detail: the Jevons Paradox works when the efficiency gain creates more demand across the broader economy. It breaks when the efficiency gain destroys the purchasing power of the people who would generate that demand.

Coal got cheaper. Factories used more coal. But the factory workers still had jobs. They still earned wages. They still spent money. The economic loop was complete.

Software is getting cheaper to build. Companies are building more software. But the people being displaced are not just software engineers. They are the white-collar professionals across every industry whose spending drives 65% of the consumer economy. When a company uses AI to cut its marketing department from 20 people to 5, those 15 people do not immediately become software architects. They downshift. They take lower-paying jobs. They spend less.

The Jevons Paradox assumes that cheaper production leads to more demand. But demand requires consumers with money. If the efficiency gains flow primarily to capital (corporate margins, stock buybacks, compute investment) rather than to labor (wages, employment, purchasing power), the paradox does not complete its loop.

This is what CitriniResearch called "Ghost GDP." Output that shows up in the national accounts but never circulates through the real economy. You can have booming software production and a shrinking consumer economy at the same time. They are not contradictions. They are two sides of the same coin.

## The Programming Exception

Here is what makes the programming data tricky: software engineers are not a representative sample of the labor market. They are the one profession that directly benefits from AI productivity tools, because their output (code) is the thing AI is making cheaper. A programmer with AI tools is dramatically more productive. That makes each programmer more valuable, not less.

But extend the lens beyond programming and the picture changes. Accountants with AI tools need fewer junior accountants. Law firms with AI tools need fewer paralegals. Marketing departments with AI tools need fewer analysts. In each case, the senior humans become more productive while the junior and mid-level roles compress.

The Jevons Paradox may be operating perfectly within software. More software is being built. More programmers are needed. But the paradox does not automatically extend to every other white-collar profession. Not every efficiency gain creates more demand for the displaced workers. Sometimes it just creates more output with fewer people.

Jeffries is right that the "jobs apocalypse" framing is overblown. History does support the pattern of creative destruction leading to new opportunities. But he is making the same mistake the doomers make, just in the opposite direction. He is extrapolating from one sector (programming) and one mechanism (Jevons) to claim the entire economy will follow the same path.

It might. But it is not guaranteed. And the variable that determines the outcome is the one almost nobody is talking about.

## The Missing Variable

Both the optimists and the pessimists are arguing about technology. Will AI be good enough to replace workers? Will it create new jobs? Will the Jevons Paradox hold?

These are the wrong questions. The right question is: will the humans entering this economy have the skills to capture the opportunities that emerge?

The Jevons Paradox creates more demand for software. It does not automatically create more demand for every displaced worker. The people who thrive in a Jevons world are the ones who can do something valuable with cheaper tools. The Jakarta filmmakers from Jeffries' scenario. The marketing consultant who 4x'd her revenue. The programmer who spends 90% of their time on architecture instead of boilerplate.

What do those people have in common? They are not executing routine tasks. They are interpreting ambiguous situations, integrating knowledge across domains, building relationships, and imagining solutions that do not yet exist.

This is the 4I Framework from my book [*After the Grind*](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B0GHT7DN7F): interpretation, integration, interpersonal connection, and imagination. The grind is getting cheaper. The skills that direct the grind are getting more valuable. The Jevons Paradox is real, but it only works for you if you have something to offer beyond the task that just got automated.

## The Trap

And this is where the Jevons Paradox becomes a trap for the unprepared.

If you are a programmer who understands systems, who can architect solutions, who can translate business needs into technical strategy, the Jevons world is your paradise. AI handles the boilerplate. You handle the judgment. Your value goes up.

If you are a programmer who can only write code that AI now writes faster, the Jevons world produces more software but does not need you to write it. The paradox creates abundance for the skilled and scarcity for the routine.

This is not a technology problem. It is an education problem.

Jeffries dismisses the doomers as people who "think of life as a zero sum game." He is right that they are wrong about the macro trend. But he is wrong to dismiss the transition cost as trivial. The people who cannot adapt are not failing because they are lazy or because they lack intelligence. They are failing because nobody taught them the skills that matter in a world where the grind is free.

The Jevons Paradox tells us there will be more opportunity, not less. But opportunity without preparation is just a spectator sport.

The question, as always, is not whether the future will be better. It is whether we are preparing people to participate in it.
