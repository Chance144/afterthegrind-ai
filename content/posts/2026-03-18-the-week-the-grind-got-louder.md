Five stories crossed my desk today. Each one, on its own, is just another headline in the endless scroll of AI news. But read them together and a pattern emerges. The grind, the repetitive, routinized, low-judgment work that has defined most professional careers for the past century, is accelerating toward obsolescence. And the institutions built around it are scrambling to keep up.

Let's walk through what happened.

## Meta: Cutting People, Buying Machines

Reuters [reported this week](https://www.reuters.com/business/world-at-work/meta-planning-sweeping-layoffs-ai-costs-mount-2026-03-14/) that Meta is planning layoffs that could affect up to 15,000 employees, roughly 20% of its global workforce. The reason? The company is redirecting resources toward AI infrastructure, with plans to spend up to $600 billion on data centers and compute by 2028.

Read that again. Six hundred billion dollars. Not on people. On machines.

Meta's spokesperson called the reports "speculative." Maybe. But the trajectory is clear. When a company that once defined its value by connecting humans decides that its future runs through silicon rather than staffing, the rest of us should pay attention. This is not a blip. It is a reallocation of how organizations think about where value is created.

## The Midwest Pivots to Automation

A [BMO survey released today](https://www.stocktitan.net/news/BMO/bmo-business-outlook-midwest-companies-put-ai-automation-and-capital-x9hvncrdbpw5.html) found that companies across Illinois, Wisconsin, Minnesota, and Indiana are moving past AI pilot programs and into full deployment. The driver is not futurism. It is desperation. Labor markets remain tight, and these manufacturers and service firms cannot find enough workers to operate at capacity.

So they are building capacity without workers. Or more precisely, they are building capacity with fewer workers who do different things. The repetitive tasks, the data entry, the quality checks that used to require a person watching a screen for eight hours, are being handed to systems that do not take breaks, do not quit, and do not need health insurance.

This is the grind disappearing in real time. Not in a San Francisco startup. In factories and offices across middle America.

## AI Job Replacement: Now With a Calculator

DQ India [published a comprehensive overview](https://www.dqindia.com/news/ai-impact-on-jobs-in-2026-what-ai-could-replace-what-will-grow-and-how-work-is-changing-11225079) of AI's impact on jobs in 2026, citing the World Economic Forum's estimate that automation could affect 22% of existing roles by 2030. McKinsey's research suggests generative AI could automate 60 to 70% of tasks involving document processing, research, and data analysis.

The article also highlights the rise of AI agents: systems that do not just answer questions but autonomously execute multi-step tasks. These agents can analyze data, organize documents, and manage workflows without constant human oversight.

This is the key shift. We have moved past "AI as a tool you use" to "AI as a colleague that works independently." The grind is not just being assisted. It is being replaced.

## One Prompt to Launch a Business

Durable, a Vancouver-based platform, [announced a major expansion](https://www.globenewswire.com/news-release/2026/03/17/3257754/0/en/Durable-Launches-End-to-End-AI-Business-Platform-for-Entrepreneurs.html) that lets entrepreneurs launch an entire business from a single prompt. Website, customer management, payments, marketing, AI agents for ongoing operations. All from one interface.

Their founder, James Clift, put it bluntly: "Traditional employment is no longer the stable choice it once was. With AI, people now have the opportunity to build successful businesses without becoming marketers, developers, or operational experts."

Over three million entrepreneurs have already used the platform. One of them, Rebecca Hoekstra, turned a neighbor's Instagram post into a fully booked cleaning business within a year. She did not hire a web developer or a marketing consultant. She used a tool that handled the grind so she could focus on her craft.

This is the after-the-grind economy in miniature. The operational friction that used to require years of learning (or hiring) is collapsing. What remains is the human part: the idea, the expertise, the relationship with the customer.

## Universities: Existential Questions, No Easy Answers

Meanwhile, in the world I inhabit daily, higher education is wrestling with what all of this means. A piece in [Minding the Campus](https://mindingthecampus.org/2026/03/17/fellowships-cant-replace-universities-ai/) argued that fellowship programs cannot replace universities, even as AI threatens to undermine the traditional value proposition of a degree. The author's point is nuanced: universities provide mass education and mass credentialing, functions that boutique alternatives simply cannot replicate at scale.

But the implicit admission is striking. The article accepts that AI poses an existential challenge to higher education. The debate is no longer "will AI change universities?" It is "what can possibly replace them if they fail to adapt?"

## What Connects All of This

Here is the thread that runs through every one of these stories. The grind, the repetitive, procedural, by-the-book work that has structured careers and institutions for generations, is being automated away. Not slowly. Not theoretically. Right now.

Meta is betting $600 billion that machines will create more value than many of its 79,000 employees. Midwest manufacturers are deploying AI because they literally cannot find humans to do the grinding work. Entrepreneurs are launching businesses without mastering the operational grind. And universities are debating their survival because the credential grind may no longer justify the cost.

So what is left?

This is the question I wrote *After the Grind* to answer. When AI handles the mechanical, the repetitive, the procedural, human value shifts to four capabilities that machines still cannot replicate.

**Interpretation**: making sense of ambiguous data, reading a room, understanding what the numbers actually mean in context.

**Integration**: connecting ideas across domains, synthesizing insights from different disciplines, seeing the whole when everyone else sees parts.

**Interpersonal connection**: building trust, navigating conflict, leading teams through uncertainty, being the person others want to work with.

**Imagination**: asking the questions that have not been asked, seeing opportunities where others see obstacles, creating something genuinely new.

Rebecca Hoekstra did not succeed because she mastered web development. She succeeded because she saw an opportunity (imagination), understood her local market (interpretation), combined cleaning expertise with business strategy (integration), and built relationships with her customers (interpersonal connection).

The Midwest companies deploying AI are not eliminating humans. They are redefining what humans do. The workers who thrive will be the ones who interpret the data the machines produce, integrate insights across systems, connect with customers and teammates, and imagine better ways to operate.

Even the university debate comes back to this. If AI can deliver information and assess basic competence, what is the university for? It is for the things that cannot be automated: the seminar discussion that changes how you think, the mentorship that shapes your career, the collaborative project that teaches you to work with people who see the world differently.

The grind is getting louder as it grinds to a halt. The question for every professional, every student, every organization is simple: what are you building that the machines cannot do?
