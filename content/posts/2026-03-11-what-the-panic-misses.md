Andrew Yang stood at a podium in Washington last month and told America that ["The Fuckening" is here](https://www.theatlantic.com/technology/2026/03/ai-layoffs-block-jack-dorsey/686304/). His word, not mine. His argument: millions of knowledge workers are about to lose their jobs, bankruptcy rates will spike, and downtown office districts will hollow out. Days later, Block (the company behind Square and Cash App) [laid off roughly 4,000 workers](https://www.theatlantic.com/technology/2026/03/ai-layoffs-block-jack-dorsey/686304/), nearly half its workforce, with CEO Jack Dorsey citing AI as the reason. "Smaller and flatter teams," he wrote, "are enabling a new way of working."

Meanwhile, the numbers keep piling up. [Across Meta, Oracle, Amazon, and other major tech firms, over 35,000 jobs have been cut worldwide so far in 2026](https://www.financialexpress.com/life/technology-ai-layoffs-2026-from-meta-oracle-to-amazon-tech-companies-cut-over-35000-jobs-worldwide-amid-ai-restructuring-4166698/), all framed as "AI restructuring." The Wall Street Journal called it the week "the dreaded AI jobs wipeout got real."

So: is this the moment we've been warning about? Sort of. But the panic, loud as it is, misses something important.

## The Gap Between Could and Does

A [new study from Anthropic](https://fortune.com/2026/03/10/will-ai-take-your-job-this-chart-in-an-economic-study-by-anthropic-may-give-you-a-hint-but-the-answer-is-complicated/) released this month offers the clearest data yet on what AI is actually doing to the labor market, as opposed to what it theoretically could do. Economists Maxim Massenkoff and Peter McCrory measured two things: "potential exposure" (how many tasks in a given job AI could theoretically handle) and "observed exposure" (how many tasks AI is actually performing right now).

The gap between those two numbers is enormous. In computer science and math occupations, theoretical exposure sits at 94%. The actual automation rate? Thirty-three percent. Office administration shows a similar pattern. Healthcare and social sciences have high potential exposure but almost no observed displacement.

This is not a story about AI being harmless. It is a story about the difference between capability and adoption, between a technology that *could* replace a task and an organization that actually does. And that difference is where human judgment lives.

## The AI-Washing Problem

Here's where it gets messy. Block's layoffs were dramatic, but several analysts have pointed out that the company [tripled its workforce during the pandemic](https://www.theatlantic.com/technology/2026/03/ai-layoffs-block-jack-dorsey/686304/) and was almost certainly due for a correction regardless of AI. The term for this is "AI-washing": using AI as a convenient, investor-friendly explanation for cuts that have more to do with bloated headcount or plain old bad decisions.

This matters because it distorts how we think about the problem. If companies blame AI for layoffs that are really about overhiring, we build policy around a phantom. If workers believe AI caused their layoff when it was actually management failure, they chase the wrong skills in the wrong direction. The narrative shapes the response, and right now the narrative is sloppy.

## Higher Ed's Fork in the Road

Which brings us to the universities. [Northwestern announced this week](https://dailynorthwestern.com/2026/03/10/campus/northwestern-announces-new-ai-major-beginning-fall-2026/) that it will offer a full undergraduate AI major starting in fall 2026. The curriculum covers machine learning, natural language processing, computer vision, and robotics. It is exactly what you would expect a top engineering school to build.

But a Washington Post opinion piece from earlier this month makes the opposite argument. [Greg Weiner, president of Assumption University, wrote](https://www.washingtonpost.com/opinions/2026/03/02/ais-best-use-is-enhancing-human-judgment-so-study-liberal-arts/) that the proliferation of AI programs in higher education represents "exactly the wrong lesson" from AI's transformation of the workplace. His thesis: AI's best use is enhancing human judgment, and the way you develop human judgment is through the liberal arts, not through more technical training.

Meanwhile, [a Chinese political advisor warned this week](http://en.people.cn/n3/2026/0311/c90000-20434846.html) that students must be guided to use AI "rationally" and avoid "intellectual laziness" as the technology reshapes education and the role of teachers.

These are not contradictory positions. They are pointing at the same reality from different angles. The question is not whether students should learn about AI. Of course they should. The question is whether learning *about* AI is sufficient preparation for a world *shaped by* AI. And the answer is obviously no.

## The 4I Framework and What Actually Matters

This is the core argument of *After the Grind*. AI is systematically removing THE GRIND: the repetitive, process-heavy, information-retrieval work that has defined most professional careers for decades. What it cannot remove, and what it makes more valuable, are the four distinctly human capabilities I call the 4I model:

**Interpretation.** AI can surface data and patterns. It cannot tell you what they mean for your specific organization, culture, or stakeholders. The Anthropic study is a perfect example. The numbers are available to everyone. The interpretation of what they mean for hiring strategy, education policy, or individual career planning requires context, experience, and judgment that no model possesses.

**Integration.** AI excels at narrow tasks. It cannot weave together insights from marketing, finance, operations, and human resources into a coherent strategy. The companies cutting thousands of workers and calling it "AI restructuring" are learning, or will learn, that removing the people who integrated across functions creates a different kind of organizational debt.

**Interpersonal connection.** No AI system builds trust. No AI system reads the room in a tense negotiation, coaches a struggling employee through a career transition, or builds the kind of relationship with a client that survives a bad quarter. The Chinese advisor's warning about "intellectual laziness" is really a warning about outsourcing the relational work of education to a tool that cannot do it.

**Imagination.** AI generates. It does not imagine. It does not look at a broken system and envision a fundamentally different one. It does not ask, "What if we stopped doing this entirely?" The most valuable people in every organization I have studied are not the ones who execute the grind efficiently. They are the ones who see what is not there yet.

## The Real Takeaway

Thirty-five thousand layoffs are real. The anxiety is justified. But the response most people are having, which is either panic or a rush to learn prompt engineering, misses the point.

The Anthropic data shows that actual AI adoption is far behind theoretical capability. The AI-washing phenomenon shows that corporate narratives are unreliable. And the higher education debate shows that even smart institutions are struggling to figure out what to teach when the ground is shifting this fast.

Here is what I tell my students: the grind is ending. That is not a threat. It is a liberation, but only if you have something to offer beyond the grind. The professionals who will thrive are not the ones who learn to use AI tools the fastest. They are the ones who develop the interpretation, integration, interpersonal, and imagination skills that make AI tools worth using in the first place.

The panic is real. The question is whether you let it drive you toward the wrong preparation, or toward the right one.
