There is a number that should keep higher education leaders awake right now: 42.5 percent.

That is the underemployment rate for college graduates as of December 2025, according to data from the [Federal Reserve Bank of New York](https://www.newyorkfed.org/research/college-labor-market#--:explore:underemployment). Nearly half of college-educated young workers are doing jobs that don't require their degrees. And that figure arrived before the real AI-driven restructuring of entry-level work got fully underway.

This week, three stories landed that together tell a coherent and uncomfortable story about where we are and where we are heading.

**The Reshaping Is Happening, Right Now**

[BCG published research](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2026/ai-will-reshape-more-jobs-than-it-replaces) concluding that over the next two to three years, 50 to 55 percent of US jobs will be reshaped by AI. Not eliminated, necessarily. Reshaped. The distinction matters, but so does the scale. Half the workforce. In two to three years. The report makes a point that cuts against the comforting narrative that AI mostly threatens low-skill repetitive work: workforce strategy, BCG argues, can no longer sit downstream of automation decisions. It has to be upstream. Embedded. Concurrent.

Meanwhile, the tech sector is not waiting for strategic clarity. [Over 73,000 tech workers lost their jobs in Q1 2026 alone](https://en.channeliam.com/2026/04/20/global-tech-layoffs-ai-job-cuts/), according to industry tracking data. More than one million tech jobs have been cut globally since 2021. Companies are reporting strong revenues while simultaneously cutting headcount. The math is not complicated: AI tools are absorbing tasks that used to require teams of people, and the savings flow directly to the bottom line. Cloud computing, software, e-commerce: the sectors hit hardest are exactly the sectors that once reliably absorbed business school graduates.

On the government side, the [General Services Administration announced plans to automate one million work hours](https://federalnewsnetwork.com/artificial-intelligence/2026/04/gsa-looks-to-automate-a-million-work-hours-after-losing-nearly-40-of-its-workforce/) using AI. The agency has already lost nearly 40 percent of its workforce under recent budget cuts. The remaining work still has to get done. Automation is the answer. No one is asking whether this is wise or whether the right checks are in place. The pressure to operate with fewer people is simply too high, and the tools are available.

**The Education Response: Well-Meaning but Late**

At the [ASU+GSV Summit this week in San Diego](https://www.govtech.com/education/higher-ed/asu-gsv-2026-preparing-students-for-the-future-of-ai-enabled-work), university presidents, workforce experts, and industry leaders gathered to discuss exactly this problem. The headline insight from the conference is worth quoting directly. Sukhwant Jhaj, Arizona State University's vice provost for academic innovation, said: "Work must live inside the curriculum, not at the end of it. Not as a capstone, not simply as an internship, but woven throughout."

That is the right instinct. But the conference also surfaced a hard truth: employers are now expecting entry-level candidates to arrive with AI fluency and years of experience. The tasks that traditionally defined early careers, note-taking, basic research, routine analysis, formatting reports, compiling data, those tasks are being absorbed by AI systems. The training ground that once existed inside entry-level jobs is disappearing. And universities are not quite sure what to put in its place.

Here is the problem. The traditional arc of a business career looked like this: you grind through the low-level work, you develop judgment through repetition, you earn your way to higher-value responsibilities over time. The grind, for all its frustration, was functional. It built pattern recognition. It built professional identity. It built the capacity to eventually do the hard, ambiguous, judgment-intensive work that actually moves organizations forward.

AI just absorbed the grind. Which means the old training ladder has a missing rung.

**What Fills the Gap: The 4I Model**

The answer is not to simulate the old grind with assignments that mimic what AI can now do in seconds. That is the equivalent of teaching people to hand-calculate square roots after the calculator exists. It is theater, not education.

The answer is to accelerate students into the work that AI genuinely cannot do well, and to train them in it rigorously and early.

That work maps onto four domains, what I call the 4I model in [After the Grind](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B0GHT7DN7F).

**Interpretation.** Data is cheap. Insight is expensive. AI can surface patterns, but a human has to decide what a pattern means for this organization, in this market, at this moment. Interpretation requires context, judgment, and the willingness to be wrong. You can't automate that, at least not yet. Students need practice making calls with incomplete information and defending them.

**Integration.** No AI system operates in isolation. The real leverage in any organization is connecting what the AI knows with what the humans know, aligning technical systems with human purposes, and translating between them. Integration is a skill of synthesis. It requires understanding multiple domains simultaneously. That is precisely what a rigorous business curriculum can build, if it tries.

**Interpersonal connection.** The ASU+GSV leaders were right that employers still rank communication, teamwork, and problem-solving as paramount. These are not soft skills in the dismissive sense. They are the hardest skills to automate because they depend on trust, presence, and the ability to read what another person actually needs, not what they said they need. Relationship-building, negotiation, conflict resolution: these are worth more now, not less.

**Imagination.** Strategy, creativity, and the ability to envision possibilities that don't yet exist: these are the highest-value capabilities in a world where execution is increasingly automated. The question is no longer "can we build this?" It is "what should we build, and why?" Students who can ask that question well, and who can bring others along with a compelling answer, will not be underemployed.

**The Bottom Line**

The 42.5 percent underemployment figure is not an accident. It is a structural signal. The economy is rewarding a narrower set of capabilities, and the traditional entry-level pathway that once broadened those capabilities is eroding fast. Universities that treat curriculum as a fixed asset are going to keep producing graduates who are technically credentialed but practically unready.

The BCG finding is worth sitting with: workforce strategy cannot sit downstream of automation. The same is true for curriculum strategy. Universities cannot wait until graduation to introduce students to the actual conditions of the labor market they are entering. Work, real work, the kind that requires interpretation, integration, connection, and imagination, has to live inside the classroom from day one.

The grind is largely gone. What we build in its place is the actual question.
