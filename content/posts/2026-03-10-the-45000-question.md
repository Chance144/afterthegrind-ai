Forty-five thousand.

That's how many tech workers lost their jobs in the first two months of 2026. According to [a new analysis from RationalFX](https://technode.global/2026/03/09/2026-tech-layoffs-reach-45000-in-march-more-than-9200-due-to-ai-and-automation-rationalfx/), more than 9,200 of those cuts were directly attributed to AI and automation. If the pace holds, we'll blow past last year's 245,000 total layoffs before the leaves change color.

Meanwhile, Amazon just [cut over 100 white-collar positions in its robotics division](https://www.techrepublic.com/article/news-amazon-robotics-layoffs-march-2026/). Read that again. The people who *built the robots* are being replaced by better robots. The company is expanding its warehouse automation fleet while shrinking the human team that designed it. If there was ever a cleaner symbol of this moment, I haven't seen it.

And yet. Today Fortune published [a sharp piece](https://fortune.com/2026/03/10/ai-killing-jobs-ceos-using-layoffs-fund-trillion-arms-race/) arguing that AI isn't actually killing jobs. CEOs are. They're using AI as cover to fund a $2.5 trillion arms race in compute infrastructure, cutting headcount not because the machines can do the work, but because investors reward the narrative. As Brad Conger of Hirtle Callaghan put it: "AI's not replacing jobs, but job cuts are funding AI expenditures." The layoffs are real. The reason behind them is murkier than the headlines suggest.

## The Anthropic List

Anthropic, the company behind Claude, released [its own exposure index this week](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/anthropic-ai-jobs-most-exposed-risk/), ranking which jobs are most vulnerable to AI disruption. Computer programmers top the list at 75% task exposure. Customer service reps sit at 70%. Market research analysts and marketing specialists: 65%.

But here's the part that matters most. Anthropic's own researchers found "limited evidence that AI has affected employment to date." The *capability* is there. The *displacement* hasn't arrived yet. We're in the gap between what AI could do and what organizations have actually deployed. That gap is where careers get renegotiated.

What caught my eye was a secondary finding: 77% of Gen Z workers now say it's important that their future job is hard to automate. More young people are turning to carpentry, plumbing, and electrical work. Not because they can't code. Because they've watched the people who can code get shown the door.

## Northwestern's Bet

On the other end of the spectrum, [Northwestern University announced today](https://dailynorthwestern.com/2026/03/10/campus/northwestern-announces-new-ai-major-beginning-fall-2026/) that it will offer a full undergraduate AI major starting this fall. The curriculum covers machine learning, natural language processing, computer vision, and "responsible deployment."

It's a reasonable move. But I want to push on it a little.

If we're training students to build AI systems, we're solving yesterday's problem. The tools are already being built. The shortage isn't in people who can train a model. The shortage is in people who know what to *do* with one. People who can look at a business problem, a community challenge, a patient, a classroom, and figure out where AI helps and where it doesn't. People who can interpret ambiguous outputs, integrate them into messy human systems, build the interpersonal trust that makes adoption possible, and imagine applications nobody has thought of yet.

That's the 4I framework I outline in *After the Grind*: Interpretation, Integration, Interpersonal Connection, and Imagination. These aren't soft skills. They're the hard skills of a post-grind economy.

## The European Counterpoint

Here's where it gets interesting. While American companies are slashing payrolls and blaming AI, [Fortune reported yesterday](https://fortune.com/2026/03/09/european-companies-not-cutting-jobs-hiring-more-with-ai-american-migration-wealth-transfer/) that European companies adopting AI are actually *hiring more workers*, not fewer. The difference isn't the technology. It's the philosophy. European firms are using AI to augment teams, not replace them.

This shouldn't surprise anyone who has studied the history of technology and labor. The outcome of automation has never been determined by the technology itself. It's determined by the choices organizations make about how to deploy it. A company that sees AI as a way to do more with fewer people will cut. A company that sees AI as a way to do *better* work with the same people will grow.

The question isn't whether AI can do your job. It's whether your employer sees you as a cost to cut or a capability to amplify.

## What This Means for Students (and Everyone Else)

I teach in a business school. My students are watching all of this happen in real time. They're reading the same headlines. Some are panicking. Some are pivoting to trades. Some are doubling down on technical skills, hoping to stay one step ahead of the machine.

Here's what I tell them: stop trying to outrun the machine. You won't. Instead, become the person the machine can't replace.

The grind. The repetitive, soul-crushing, task-oriented labor that defined white-collar work for a century. That's what AI is eating. Data entry. Boilerplate code. First-pass research. Rote customer interactions. Nobody should mourn the loss of that work. It wasn't making anyone's life better.

What remains is the work that requires judgment, context, trust, and creativity. The work where you have to sit across from another human being and figure out what they actually need, not just what they typed into a search bar. The work where you synthesize conflicting data streams into a recommendation your team can act on. The work where you imagine something that doesn't exist yet and convince other people to build it with you.

That work isn't going away. If anything, it's becoming more valuable as everything around it gets automated.

Forty-five thousand people lost their jobs in two months. That's painful and real. But the right response isn't to hide from AI or to worship it. The right response is to ask a harder question: now that the grind is ending, what are you actually good at?

The answer to that question is your career.
