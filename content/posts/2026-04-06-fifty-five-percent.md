Here is a number worth sitting with for a moment: 55 percent.

That is [BCG's estimate](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2026/ai-will-reshape-more-jobs-than-it-replaces) of how many US jobs will be substantially reshaped by AI over the next two to three years. Not eliminated. Reshaped. The framing matters, because it tells you something the panic-driven headlines usually miss. This is not primarily a story about robots taking jobs. It is a story about jobs changing so fast that the people doing them barely recognize their own roles anymore.

At roughly the same moment BCG published that figure, a Yale economist named Pascual Restrepo dropped a working paper through the [National Bureau of Economic Research](http://www.nber.org/papers/w34423) with the blunt title "We Won't Be Missed." His [argument, reported in Fortune](https://fortune.com/2026/04/04/ai-jobs-future-not-important-enough-to-be-automated-yale/), is counterintuitive: most human jobs won't be automated by AGI, not because humans are irreplaceable, but because most jobs simply aren't important enough to justify the compute. AI will chase the high-value bottlenecks. Reducing existential risks. Cracking fusion energy. Mapping protein structures. The rest of us? We'll probably keep our jobs. We'll just matter a lot less to the growth curve.

That is not the reassuring news it sounds like.

And then there is this: [according to a new Gallup and Lumina Foundation survey](https://www.insidehighered.com/news/quick-takes/2026/04/02/ai-pushing-students-consider-changing-majors-data-shows), 47 percent of college students have considered switching their major because of AI. Nearly half. They are scared, and they are not entirely wrong to be. Between 2022 and 2025, early-career workers in AI-exposed occupations, think software development and clerical work, saw a 16 percent relative employment decline. The entry-level pipeline is already narrowing. Students are noticing.

So here is where we are: the majority of jobs are changing shape, the ones that aren't changing may not be generating much value anyway, and the next generation of workers is anxious enough about all of this that they are reconsidering the degrees they just enrolled in. What is the right response to that?

If you spend any time in higher education right now, you will find two dominant answers. The first is denial: everything will be fine, AI is just a tool, stop worrying. The second is credential proliferation: add a data analytics requirement here, a prompt engineering badge there, and declare the curriculum "AI-ready." Neither of these is serious.

The University of Chicago is trying something more serious. This week, UChicago announced a [$50 million gift](https://www.insidehighered.com/news/quick-takes/2026/04/06/uchicago-receives-50m-gift-hire-ai-expert-faculty) to launch the Mansueto Faculty of Mind and Machine Challenge, with a goal of raising nearly $200 million to recruit 20 scholars who exemplify AI integration across disciplines: arts, social sciences, medicine, business, law. The key word there is interdisciplinary. Not AI as a department. AI as a lens that cuts across everything.

That is the right instinct. But it still only addresses the supply side of the problem. It funds the faculty. It does not automatically tell students what to do with the degree they are reconsidering.

So let me be direct about what I think students, and their professors, and their employers should actually be building right now.

The BCG report makes it clear that most jobs are being reshaped, not eliminated. What that means in practice is that the repetitive, routine, data-entry-level, first-draft-producing components of those jobs are going to AI. The grind, to use a term I care about, is leaving the building. The question is what is left after it goes.

What is left is interpretation, integration, interpersonal connection, and imagination. Four things. I call them the 4I model, and they are not buzzwords. They are the specific human capacities that AI cannot replicate at scale, and they are the capacities that become more valuable as AI absorbs everything else.

Interpretation is the ability to take AI-generated outputs and ask whether they are actually right, actually relevant, and actually applicable to the specific messy situation in front of you. AI gives you the analysis. You decide what to do with it.

Integration is the ability to connect information across domains, to see how the marketing problem is also an operations problem, how the hiring decision is also a culture decision, how the supply chain disruption is also a customer trust story. AI is excellent at depth within a domain. Humans are still better at breadth across them.

Interpersonal connection is obvious but underweighted. Restrepo's paper makes an implicit point that is worth naming explicitly: when the economy sorts into "compute-handled" and "human-handled," the things that remain human-handled will disproportionately involve other humans. Coaching. Negotiating. Motivating. Managing. Selling in complex, high-stakes environments. These skills atrophy when you skip the entry-level reps that used to build them. That is the real cost of the 16 percent employment decline among early-career workers: not just those jobs, but the developmental scaffolding those jobs provided.

Imagination is the capacity to generate new directions, not just optimize existing ones. Restrepo argues that AI will be directed at bottleneck work critical for future progress. Humans who can see what the bottlenecks even are, who can identify the problem worth solving before the AI is pointed at it, will matter enormously. That is not a technical skill. It is a creative and strategic one.

Here is what I would say to the 47 percent of college students considering switching their majors: the anxiety is understandable, but the question you should be asking is not "which major is safe from AI." That question has no good answer. Ask instead: "Am I building the capacity to interpret, integrate, connect, and imagine?" If yes, you are on the right track regardless of what is on your diploma.

And for universities: a $50 million interdisciplinary AI initiative is not a bad place to start. But the goal should not be producing people who know how to use AI. The goal should be producing people who know what to do after AI has done its part. That is a different curriculum. It is harder to design. It is the only one that will hold its value.

The grind is ending. That is a good thing. But "after the grind" is not a vacation. It is a harder, more human, more consequential kind of work. The institutions that understand that will produce graduates who thrive in the reshaped 55 percent. The ones that don't will keep producing people for jobs that no longer exist in the form they trained for.
