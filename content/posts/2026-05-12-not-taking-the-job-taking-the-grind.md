Eighty-nine percent. That is the share of 2026 college graduates who believe AI could replace their entry-level jobs, up from 64 percent just one year ago, [according to Monster's 2026 Graduate AI Readiness Report](https://www.linkforcounselors.com/89-of-grads-fear-ai-could-replace-entry-level-jobs-up-from-64-in-2025/). That is not a statistic you shrug off. That is a generation stepping onto a campus stage in May, diploma in hand, genuinely unsure whether the career they trained for still exists.

They are right that something fundamental is shifting. They are wrong about what, exactly, is disappearing.

Let me explain the distinction, because it matters enormously for how you spend the next decade of your professional life.

**The Cloudflare Signal**

This week, Cloudflare announced it is cutting approximately 20 percent of its workforce, roughly 1,100 people, and [explicitly tied the decision to AI adoption reshaping operations](https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/08/cloudflare-says-ai-made-1100-jobs-obsolete-even-as-revenue-hit-a-record-high/). The announcement landed while the company was reporting record-high revenue. Read that sentence again: record revenue, 20 percent fewer people. That is the new math of the AI era, and it is going to appear in more earnings calls before the year is out.

Here is the uncomfortable truth that tends to get lost in the headline: Cloudflare did not eliminate the work. They eliminated the friction. The repetitive processing, the ticket-routing, the first-pass analysis, the documentation that used to require human hours now runs on infrastructure that never sleeps and does not call in sick. The strategic thinking, the customer relationships, the architectural decisions, the judgment calls: those stayed.

This is not a Cloudflare story. It is a structural story. And [Bloomberg's economists are already warning](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-11/ai-related-job-displacement-isn-t-being-planned-for-enough-economists-say) that neither companies nor governments are planning adequately for what comes next.

**What CNN Got Right (and Did Not Say Loudly Enough)**

A CNN piece published this week carried a headline that deserves more attention: ["AI isn't actually 'taking' your job. Here's what's happening instead."](https://www.cnn.com/2026/05/10/tech/ai-taking-jobs) The argument, drawn from a growing body of workplace research, is that companies are automating certain parts of jobs rather than replacing entire positions.

That framing is accurate. It is also cold comfort unless you understand the implication. When AI absorbs the parts of a job that used to require entry-level humans, the entry-level tier collapses. The junior analyst who spent three years building spreadsheets before earning the trust to interpret them is now facing a world where the spreadsheets build themselves. The question the organization is now asking is: do we still need those three years? Or can we hire someone who already knows how to interpret?

This is what I call the collapse of the on-ramp. The grind, meaning the repetitive, rote, foundational labor that used to teach young professionals how business actually works, is being absorbed by machines. That is not a bug. It is a feature. But it requires a serious rethinking of how you become competent, how you demonstrate value, and what skills actually belong on a resume.

**The Question the New Yorker Is Really Asking**

The New Yorker ran a piece this week titled ["Will AI Make College Obsolete?"](https://www.newyorker.com/news/fault-lines/will-ai-make-college-obsolete) and it is worth reading in full. The piece captures the anxiety: funding cuts, AI-assisted cheating, and more than one in four students already doubting whether tuition was worth it. The uncertainty is real. More than 40 percent of college students are not completing their degrees, and this is happening at the same moment that entry-level white-collar jobs are being restructured out of existence.

Here is my answer to the New Yorker's question: no, AI will not make college obsolete. But it will make certain versions of college irrelevant. The version that promises employment in exchange for rote credential-seeking: that version is in serious trouble. The version that develops judgment, communication, curiosity, and the ability to synthesize complex information across domains: that version has never been more valuable.

The distinction matters, and higher education has not finished having that argument with itself.

**The Other Threat Nobody Wants to Talk About**

The Guardian ran a sharp piece this week arguing that [AI's real threat is not job loss but worker control and surveillance](https://www.theguardian.com/technology/2026/may/11/ai-worker-control-surveillance). The argument is worth sitting with. When AI is used to train and augment higher-paid workers while being deployed to surveil and manage lower-paid ones, the technology amplifies existing inequality rather than democratizing opportunity. That is not a technological outcome. That is a choice. Organizations make it, and institutions enable it.

This is why the framing of "AI versus jobs" misses the real ethical terrain. The question is not whether AI takes your job. The question is whether AI makes your work more human or less.

**The Four Skills That Survive All of This**

In my book, I argue that the future of business careers is organized around four capabilities that AI cannot replicate at scale: interpretation, integration, interpersonal connection, and imagination. The 4I model is not optimistic window-dressing. It is an observation about what actually differentiates human output from machine output, and it is holding up well against the news of this week.

Interpretation means reading a data output and knowing what it actually means for a client, a strategy, or a market. Anyone can run the model. Not everyone can tell the room what the model is missing.

Integration means connecting information across disciplines, experiences, and contexts to reach a conclusion that is genuinely new. This is the skill that makes a business school education worth something: not the accounting course in isolation, but the student who can connect the accounting to the marketing to the organizational behavior and see the full picture.

Interpersonal connection means the ability to build trust, navigate conflict, read a room, and hold a relationship through difficulty. AI can simulate this. People can tell the difference.

Imagination means the willingness to propose something that does not yet exist and make a case for it. This is the rarest skill in any organization, and it has never appeared on a job description.

The 89 percent of graduates who are afraid should be afraid of the right things. Not that AI will eliminate their careers, but that they might arrive unprepared to demonstrate value in the terms that matter now. That preparation is not about learning another tool. It is about developing judgment. And judgment is built through doing hard, ambiguous work in high-stakes situations, not through optimizing a workflow.

The grind is ending. The work is not.

The graduates who understand that distinction are going to be fine.
