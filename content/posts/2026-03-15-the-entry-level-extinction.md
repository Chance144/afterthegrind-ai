Here's a question nobody in a career center wants to answer honestly: what happens to the bottom rung of the corporate ladder when AI saws it off?

This week gave us several data points that, taken together, paint a picture that's both alarming and clarifying. A [Dallas Fed report](https://www.telegraphherald.com/news/business/article_28dfb9a6-0f41-57c1-9bc6-141155d23db0.html) found that AI is making it measurably harder to land entry-level jobs. Not hypothetically. Not in five years. Now. Meanwhile, [Anthropic released a study](https://www.investopedia.com/anthropic-identifies-jobs-most-exposed-to-ai-risks-is-your-occupation-affected-11922109) ranking which occupations face the highest exposure to AI automation, and entry-level hiring in the most exposed roles has already dropped 14%. Over at [Morgan Stanley's TMT Conference](https://fortune.com/2026/03/12/ai-jobs-future-morgan-stanley-tmt-conference-ceos/), analysts projected that middle and upper-middle income professionals whose jobs are most exposed to automation will see their spending power decline, while high-income workers riding the AI wave pull further ahead.

And then there's the education side. The [Guardian published a deep look](https://www.theguardian.com/technology/ng-interactive/2026/mar/10/ai-impact-professors-students-learning) at professors scrambling to save critical thinking in an age of AI, with one academic warning of a future split: elite students getting traditional liberal arts education, everyone else getting "a degraded, soulless form of vocational training administered by AI instructors." At the same time, the [Washington Post ran an opinion piece](https://www.washingtonpost.com/opinions/2026/03/02/ais-best-use-is-enhancing-human-judgment-so-study-liberal-arts/) by Assumption University president Greg Weiner arguing that AI's best use is enhancing human judgment, and that higher education is drawing exactly the wrong lesson by proliferating AI-specific programs of study instead of doubling down on the liberal arts.

If you connect the dots, the picture is striking. And it maps directly onto a framework I've been developing called the 4I model.

## The Bottom Rung Problem

Let's start with what's actually happening in the labor market. The traditional career path in business has always started with the grind. You get the entry-level job, you do the repetitive work nobody else wants to do, you learn the ropes, and over time you earn more responsibility. It's not glamorous, but it works. Or it did.

The Dallas Fed data confirms what many hiring managers already know: companies are using AI to handle the exact tasks that used to define entry-level positions. Data entry. Basic analysis. Report generation. First-pass research. Scheduling. Correspondence drafting. These aren't theoretical capabilities. They're deployed, operational, and cheaper than a junior hire with benefits.

Anthropic's study puts numbers on the exposure. The jobs most vulnerable aren't just low-skill positions. They're the structured, process-oriented roles that follow clear rules and produce predictable outputs. In other words, they're the roles where the work is primarily grind.

Here's the thing most commentary misses: this isn't just an employment problem. It's a development problem. Entry-level jobs have always been more than a paycheck. They've been the training ground where young professionals develop judgment, learn organizational culture, build relationships, and figure out how to navigate ambiguity. Remove the bottom rung and you don't just leave people unemployed. You leave them undeveloped.

## The Split That's Coming

The Guardian piece about professors and critical thinking captures the education version of this same tension. When students can generate passable essays, analyses, and even code with a few prompts, what exactly are we teaching? What's the exam testing? The professors interviewed aren't Luddites. They're genuinely wrestling with how to cultivate the kind of thinking that AI can't replicate.

And the worry about a two-tier system is real. If the response to AI in education is to lean harder into vocational AI training for most students while preserving the old-school liberal arts experience for the elite, we'll have perfectly prepared a generation of workers to compete with machines on the machines' home turf. That's a losing strategy.

Greg Weiner's Washington Post argument is the counterpoint, and I think he's closer to right than most of higher ed wants to admit. If AI handles the technical execution, then the premium shifts to judgment, interpretation, ethical reasoning, and the ability to ask good questions. Those are liberal arts skills. Philosophy. History. Literature. Rhetoric. The disciplines that teach you to think about thinking.

## Where the 4I Model Fits

In *After the Grind*, I argue that as AI removes the grind from work, human value shifts to four capabilities: Interpretation, Integration, Interpersonal Connection, and Imagination. These aren't soft skills in the way people usually mean. They're the hard skills of the post-grind economy.

**Interpretation** is the ability to make meaning from data, context, and ambiguity. AI can summarize a dataset. It can't tell you what it means for your specific organization, culture, or market position. That requires judgment born from experience, domain knowledge, and the kind of critical thinking those professors are trying to protect.

**Integration** is connecting dots across domains. The Morgan Stanley analysis is a perfect example of why this matters. Their projection that AI will create a spending gap between high-income and middle-income workers isn't just a data point. It's a signal that requires someone to integrate economic analysis, workforce policy, consumer behavior, and social dynamics into a coherent response. AI can model each piece. Humans have to decide what the picture means and what to do about it.

**Interpersonal Connection** is where the entry-level extinction hits hardest. If young professionals never go through the messy, human process of learning to work with difficult colleagues, navigate office politics, build trust with clients, and read a room, they'll lack the relational intelligence that no amount of AI training can substitute. The grind taught interpersonal skills almost by accident. We need to teach them on purpose now.

**Imagination** is the capacity to envision what doesn't yet exist. It's not creativity in the decorative sense. It's the ability to look at a landscape being reshaped by automation and see opportunity rather than just threat. China's government, as [Reuters reported this week](https://www.reuters.com/business/world-at-work/china-pins-hopes-society-wide-ai-push-add-jobs-rejuvenate-economy-2026-03-10/), is betting that AI adoption will create more jobs than it destroys. Whether that bet pays off depends entirely on whether their workforce can imagine new roles, new industries, and new forms of value that don't exist yet.

## So What Do We Do?

If you're a student right now, the worst thing you can do is optimize for a job description that was written last year. Those descriptions are being rewritten by AI, literally and figuratively. Instead, invest in the capabilities that compound over time: learning to interpret complexity, connecting ideas across boundaries, building genuine human relationships, and practicing the discipline of original thought.

If you're an educator, resist the temptation to turn your curriculum into an AI training program. Yes, students need AI literacy. But the far bigger gap is in the skills AI can't do. Teach argumentation. Teach ethical reasoning. Teach students to sit with ambiguity instead of rushing to the first plausible answer.

If you're a manager, recognize that your entry-level pipeline is drying up and plan accordingly. Mentorship, apprenticeship models, and deliberate skill development aren't nice-to-haves anymore. They're essential infrastructure for building the next generation of leaders who can do what the machines can't.

The grind is ending. That's not a tragedy. It's a transition. But transitions only go well when we're honest about what's being lost and intentional about what we build in its place. The liberal arts, it turns out, might be the most practical education we have. Not because they teach you to code. Because they teach you to think.

And thinking, it turns out, is the last competitive advantage that's actually durable.
