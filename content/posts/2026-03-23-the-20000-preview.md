HSBC is reportedly weighing cuts that could eliminate roughly [20,000 jobs](https://www.reuters.com/business/world-at-work/hsbc-mulls-job-cuts-that-could-impact-around-20000-roles-bloomberg-news-reports-2026-03-19/) over the next several years. That is around 10 percent of the bank's global workforce. The target: middle- and back-office roles that can be automated using AI. Analysts at eFinancialCareers noted almost immediately that Goldman Sachs and Citi could follow the same playbook.

Same week, Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei [told Time magazine](https://time.com/article/2026/03/11/anthropic-claude-disruptive-company-pentagon/) that AI could displace half of all entry-level white-collar jobs within one to five years and urged leaders to stop "sugar-coating" what is coming. And a [Washington Post analysis](https://www.washingtonpost.com/technology/interactive/2026/jobs-most-affected-ai-automation/) mapped it out in uncomfortable detail: computer programming, financial analysis, marketing, and customer service sit at the top of the exposure list.

If you are a business student, or managing one, this week should feel like a cold splash of water.

But here is what I want to argue: the alarming headlines are only half the story. The other half contains something genuinely useful, if you know how to read it.

**The Grind Was Always the Most Vulnerable Part**

When I wrote *After the Grind*, I made a specific argument about what AI removes from work. It does not remove judgment. It does not remove relationship. It does not remove creativity. What it removes, systematically and relentlessly, is the grind: the repetitive cognitive tasks that used to consume most of a professional's time.

Spreadsheet reconciliation. First-draft emails. Compliance documentation. Data entry. Summarizing reports that nobody wanted to write in the first place.

HSBC is not cutting 20,000 humans because machines are smarter. They are cutting those roles because the machines can now do the grind cheaply, accurately, and at scale. The jobs targeted are precisely the jobs built around routine cognitive labor. That is not a coincidence. It is the point.

The [Reuters report](https://www.reuters.com/business/world-at-work/companies-cutting-jobs-investments-shift-toward-ai-2026-03-19/) on broader corporate job cuts echoes the same logic: companies are not investing in AI because they want fewer people. They are investing because they want fewer hours spent on low-value, high-volume tasks. The reallocation is ruthless. And it is accelerating.

**The Irony in the Research**

Here is a finding worth sitting with. A [recent analysis from Allwork.Space](https://allwork.space/2026/03/the-workers-most-threatened-by-ai-may-also-be-the-ones-best-positioned-to-survive-it/) drew on multiple data sources to make a counterintuitive claim: workers in AI-exposed fields are often outperforming peers in less-exposed occupations. Being adjacent to AI, it turns out, can be protective. The workers who panic and run may be doing the opposite of what the moment requires.

That pattern points to something real. The people who learn to work *with* AI tools rather than around them are picking up capabilities that complement automation rather than compete with it. The ones who dig in, who learn to ask better questions of AI systems, who use freed-up time to do the higher-order work, are not being displaced. They are being promoted.

This is the distinction that gets lost in the noise. Automation does not flatten a profession. It stratifies it. The floor gets removed. The ceiling rises.

**What the CHROs Are Actually Asking For**

A [new survey of HR executives](https://www.morningstar.com/news/pr-newswire/20260320ph15371/2026-survey-reveals-ai-dominates-focus-for-hr-executives-as-uncertainty-abounds) conducted with the Darla Moore School of Business found that AI dominates the agenda for corporate human resources leaders in 2026. But the specific capabilities they are chasing are revealing. The survey highlights demand for AI governance skills, executive communication, and cross-functional leadership.

Notice what is on that list. Notice what is not.

Nobody is asking for faster data entry. Nobody is asking for workers who can write a better first-draft memo than ChatGPT. What CHROs want, in a world where the grind is automated, are people who can interpret results, integrate AI outputs into real decisions, lead humans through complexity, and imagine new directions.

That is exactly the 4I model I lay out in *After the Grind*: Interpretation, Integration, Interpersonal connection, and Imagination. Those four capabilities do not get cheaper when AI gets smarter. They get more valuable. Every job that AI simplifies creates new demand for the judgment required to use it well.

**The Structural Problem for Business Schools**

Here is the uncomfortable part. Most business curricula are still built around the grind.

Students spend four years learning how to do the financial models, write the market analyses, run the regressions, and format the deliverables that AI can now produce in seconds. That was the right curriculum for 2010. It is the wrong curriculum for 2026.

Dario Amodei is not wrong about the entry-level exposure. The question is whether entry-level workers arrive with skills that justify their place above the automation floor, or whether they are trained for a layer of work that simply no longer exists.

What should replace it? Courses built around interpretation: how do you evaluate an AI-generated analysis for bias, for missing context, for the assumptions baked into its outputs? Courses built around integration: how do you redesign a business process that suddenly has an AI agent in the middle of it? Courses built around interpersonal connection: how do you lead a team through a restructuring that nobody wanted? Courses built around imagination: how do you see opportunities that the AI cannot see because it only knows what has already happened?

These are not soft skills. They are the hardest skills to build and the hardest to automate. That is precisely why they matter.

**A Closing Thought on the 20,000**

The 20,000 jobs HSBC may eliminate represent real people making real mortgage payments. This should not be minimized. Transitions of this scale are genuinely disruptive and the human cost is real.

But the question I keep asking is this: what were those jobs training people to become? If the answer is "better at the grind," then automation was always going to arrive here eventually. The only real surprise is the timeline.

The version of this story that nobody is writing is the one where those same 20,000 people, given the right reskilling, could be doing work that AI cannot touch. Interpretation of complex financial situations. Client relationships that depend on trust and not just accuracy. Integration of AI tools into new kinds of service delivery. Imagining products and processes that do not yet exist.

That story is harder to write. It requires investment, and policy, and better educational architecture than most institutions have built. But it is the only version of this story worth actually believing in.
