There's a phrase making the rounds this week that I can't stop thinking about: "chaos tsunami."

It comes from [a piece on Atlassian's decision to cut 1,600 employees](https://www.hrnewsfeed.com/atlassians-ai-job-cuts-spark-warnings-of-a-chaos-tsunami-for-the-workforce/), roughly 10% of its workforce, to fund AI investment. Experts warn that companies slashing headcount to buy AI tools, without equally investing in their remaining people, are heading straight into a wall of hollowed-out talent pipelines, costly misfires, and organizational chaos. The cuts aren't happening because these companies are struggling. They're happening because the companies are profitable and want to become more so.

Atlassian isn't alone. [A running tally from Business Insider](https://www.businessinsider.com/list-companies-replacing-human-employees-with-ai-layoffs-workforce-reductions) shows that Block, HP, Meta, and a growing list of tech firms have openly linked recent layoffs to AI automation. Block cut roughly 40% of its workforce in February. Meta is reportedly eyeing cuts of more than 20%. The common thread: record profits, shrinking headcounts.

Meanwhile, the workers watching all of this are not feeling great. A new survey covered by [CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/03/16/ai-workplace-friction.html) found that while employers say AI makes workers faster, it's also creating what the researchers call "friction and mistrust." The MetLife study, drawn from surveys of thousands of employees and benefits decision-makers, reveals a workplace caught between two narratives. The efficiency story that leadership tells, and the anxiety story that employees actually live. Experts quoted in the piece emphasize that how companies introduce AI matters as much as the technology itself. Dumping tools on people without preparing them for the transition is a recipe for resentment.

And then there's the data point that sits heaviest: [63% of workers now say AI will make the workplace feel less human](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/63-of-workers-say-ai-will-make-the-workplace-feel-less-human-in-2026-302713735.html). Not slightly less human. Significantly or somewhat less human. Nearly half believe AI skills will matter in some roles but not most. Only 18% expect them to be universally required. What workers are sensing, correctly, is that something fundamental is shifting beneath their feet. And nobody is giving them a clear picture of what the ground will look like when it stops moving.

Here is where the conventional narrative falls apart. Most of the panic coverage treats this as a binary: either AI takes your job or it doesn't. But [a fascinating piece from AllWork.Space](https://allwork.space/2026/03/the-workers-most-threatened-by-ai-may-also-be-the-ones-best-positioned-to-survive-it/), drawing on the Washington Post's new [interactive analysis of AI-vulnerable jobs](https://www.washingtonpost.com/technology/interactive/2026/jobs-most-affected-ai-automation/), makes a counterintuitive argument. The workers most exposed to AI disruption may also be the ones best positioned to survive it. Why? Because the same factors that put white-collar workers in AI's crosshairs (education, digital fluency, access to strong job markets) also make career transitions more feasible for them. It's the workers without those advantages who face the steepest climb.

This is precisely the argument I make in *After the Grind*. The question was never "will AI take jobs?" It was always "what kind of value will humans provide once the grind work disappears?" And the answer isn't some vague platitude about creativity. It's specific. It's the 4I model: Interpretation, Integration, Interpersonal Connection, and Imagination.

Look at what's actually happening in these stories. Atlassian cuts 1,600 people. But experts warn the real cost will come from losing the institutional knowledge, the cross-functional relationships, the interpretive capacity that experienced workers carry. That's not a technology problem. That's an integration and interpersonal connection problem. No AI agent is going to replicate the engineer who knows which Slack channels to check when a deployment goes sideways, or the product manager who can read a room and know when a client meeting is going off the rails.

The MetLife survey reveals friction and mistrust. That's an interpersonal connection problem. Companies are treating AI adoption as a technology rollout when it's fundamentally a change management challenge. The organizations that win won't be the ones with the best AI tools. They'll be the ones whose leaders can interpret what the data means for their specific teams, integrate AI into existing workflows without breaking trust, and maintain the interpersonal connections that keep organizations functional during periods of massive change.

And then there's the imagination gap. [John Quiggin, writing in The Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com/commentisfree/2026/mar/16/ai-job-layoffs-push-for-shorter-working-hours), argues that AI layoffs should revive the push for shorter working hours. His case is straightforward: if AI genuinely produces real productivity improvements, workers should share in those benefits. But that requires imagination about what work could look like, not just what it does look like. It requires leaders who can envision a world where increased productivity doesn't automatically translate to fewer jobs, but instead to better ones. That's imagination in action, and it's desperately scarce in boardrooms right now.

Higher education is feeling this pressure too. Mark Scott, Vice-Chancellor of the University of Sydney, [identified three forces reshaping universities](https://www.afr.com/work-and-careers/education/the-three-forces-writing-the-future-of-higher-education-20260315-p5oalr): the changing value of credentials, the rise of generative AI, and the urgent need to rebuild human connection. Read that last one again. The leader of one of Australia's most prestigious universities isn't calling for more coding bootcamps or AI literacy certificates. He's saying universities need to double down on the thing that makes them irreplaceable: bringing human beings together to learn from each other in ways that no algorithm can replicate.

That's the 4I model in a nutshell. The grind, the repetitive cognitive labor that used to justify a lot of white-collar salaries, is being automated. That's real, it's accelerating, and pretending otherwise is dangerous. But the chaos tsunami that experts are warning about isn't coming from too much AI. It's coming from too little humanity. Companies that cut workers to buy tools without investing in the human capabilities that make those tools useful are going to discover, painfully, that efficiency without interpretation is just fast failure.

The workers who thrive through this transition won't be the ones who learn the most prompts. They'll be the ones who can do what the machines can't: interpret ambiguous situations, integrate insights across domains, build and maintain trust with other humans, and imagine possibilities that don't yet exist in any training dataset.

That's not a comforting platitude. It's a strategic framework. And for my students, my colleagues, and anyone reading this who felt a knot in their stomach when they saw the latest layoff headlines, it's also a roadmap.

The grind is ending. What you build in its place is entirely up to you.
