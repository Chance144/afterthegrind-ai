There is a number worth sitting with this week: 45,000.

That is how many tech sector workers have lost their jobs so far in March 2026. According to [a new report from OpenTools](https://opentools.ai/news/2026-tech-layoffs-hit-45000-in-march-ai-and-automation-take-the-lead), more than 9,200 of those cuts are directly attributable to AI and automation. Amazon, Google, and Microsoft are not laying people off because business is bad. They are laying people off because the work those people were doing can now be done without them.

Over 52% of March's tech cuts are linked to AI. Let that land.

And the tech sector is not even the most interesting part of the story.

**The Manager Is Next**

Workforce analytics firm Gloat recently published its [AI Workforce Trends for 2026](https://gloat.com/blog/ai-workforce-trends/), and one finding stopped me mid-scroll. Gartner projects that by 2026, 20% of organizations will use AI to flatten their organizational structures, eliminating more than half of current middle management positions. Not trim. Not reduce. Eliminate more than half.

Middle management has always been, at its core, an information relay system. Managers gathered data from individual contributors, synthesized it, and passed it up. They translated strategy downward and reported results upward. That is, almost without exception, grind work: rule-based, text-heavy, workflow-driven. Exactly the work that AI does better, faster, and cheaper.

Al Jazeera captured the shift neatly in a piece this month titled [The New Boss at Work May Not Be Human](https://www.aljazeera.com/economy/2026/3/8/the-new-boss-at-work-may-not-be). Companies are experimenting with AI agents that schedule, report, delegate, and monitor, reshaping office hierarchies in ways that feel like science fiction but are showing up in real org charts right now.

This is not a distant threat. It is a restructuring underway.

**Higher Education Is Not Watching from the Sidelines**

Inside Higher Ed ran a thought-provoking column this week asking [what AI says are the most vulnerable jobs in higher education](https://www.insidehighered.com/opinion/columns/online-trending-now/2026/03/18/what-ai-says-are-most-vulnerable-jobs-higher-ed). The author prompted three major AI systems and found remarkable agreement: the most vulnerable positions are not the most prestigious ones. They are the most routine, rules-based, and workflow-driven ones. Admissions processing, financial aid administration, academic advising for standard degree pathways, compliance reporting. All of it highly automatable.

The insight that stuck with me: agentic AI is more likely to reconfigure jobs and shrink headcount in selected functions than to eliminate whole professions at once. It will not look like mass layoffs. It will look like positions that simply are not refilled when people leave. Attrition as strategy.

Separately, a comprehensive [EDUCAUSE report on the impact of AI on work in higher education](https://www.educause.edu/research/2026/the-impact-of-ai-on-work-in-higher-education) found that while more than 70% of surveyed higher education professionals now use AI tools daily or weekly, over half also express significant concern about the risks. The technology is here. The clarity about what it means for careers is not.

**The Atlantic Looks Backward to Look Forward**

The Atlantic's March cover story, [America Isn't Ready for What AI Will Do to Jobs](https://www.theatlantic.com/magazine/2026/03/ai-economy-labor-market-transformation/685731/), opens with a striking historical parallel. In 1869, Massachusetts created the nation's first Bureau of Statistics of Labor because the Industrial Revolution was transforming work faster than anyone could track it. They tried counting because conscience was not enough.

The parallel to today is uncomfortable and exact. We are again living through a structural shift, not a cyclical dip. The Second Industrial Revolution automated physical labor. This one is automating cognitive labor: the reading, the sorting, the synthesizing, the reporting, the drafting. The Atlantic's reporting notes that Wall Street now expects companies to replace human labor with AI, and executives know that if they do not move, they will be the ones who lose their jobs. It is a race logic that does not wait for policy to catch up.

**So What Is Actually Happening Here**

Let me put it plainly. What we are watching is the systematic elimination of the grind.

I wrote an entire book about this because I believed the grind was ending. The events of March 2026 have compressed that timeline considerably. The grind, meaning the repetitive, rule-following, information-processing work that filled most business careers for most of the twentieth century, is being automated at scale. Not eventually. Now.

The World Economic Forum, as cited in the Gloat report, projects that by 2030, 22% of all jobs will be disrupted. 92 million roles will be displaced. 170 million new ones will be created. Net gain of 78 million. Those numbers sound optimistic until you ask the next question: who gets the new roles? Workers with AI skills are already commanding wage premiums up to 56% higher than peers without them, according to PwC's 2025 Global AI Jobs Barometer. The gap is not closing. It is widening.

**What the 4I Model Actually Predicts**

The framework I developed in After the Grind argues that when AI removes the grind, human value concentrates in four domains: Interpretation, Integration, Interpersonal connection, and Imagination. Let me be concrete about how that maps to what is happening right now.

Interpretation is the ability to take AI-generated output and determine what it actually means in a specific context. A middle manager who spent their career scheduling meetings and compiling status reports has not been developing interpretation skills. That is the harsh truth. The worker who survives this transition is the one who has been asking "what does this data tell us that the model cannot see?"

Integration is the capacity to connect insights across disciplines, functions, and contexts. AI is very good within a domain. It is far weaker at the seams, at the places where marketing meets operations, where legal meets product, where culture meets strategy. Humans who can work those seams are the ones still employed in 2028.

Interpersonal connection is the irreducible human element. The client who trusts you. The team that performs because of you. The negotiation that closes because you read the room. These cannot be automated, not because the technology is not capable enough, but because the human on the other side of the table requires a human presence to fully engage.

Imagination, finally, is the capacity to ask questions that the data does not contain. To see what could exist rather than what does. To invent the frame, not just fill it in.

**The Hard Question for Business Educators**

Here is what I keep coming back to. Higher education is not immune to this restructuring. My own institution, like every university, has administrative layers built on information relay and process compliance. That work is automatable. The question is not whether AI will reshape university employment. It already is.

The harder question is what we are teaching students to do. If the careers we are preparing them for are built primarily on the grind, we are preparing them for work that will not exist by the time they are mid-career. Business education has to stop treating AI as a tool to discuss in one elective and start treating the 4I framework as the organizing logic of an entire curriculum.

Interpretation. Integration. Interpersonal connection. Imagination.

Those are not soft skills. They are survival skills. And right now, most business programs are still measuring students on the things that AI can already do better.

The grind was the job. It is not anymore. That is both the crisis and the opportunity, and how we respond in the next few years will determine which one it becomes.
