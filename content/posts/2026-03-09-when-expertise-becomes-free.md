Vinod Khosla dropped a line this week that should make every knowledge worker stop scrolling: ["Almost all expertise globally will be free."](https://www.storyboard18.com/brand-makers/almost-all-expertise-globally-will-be-free-vinod-khosla-questions-wages-in-ai-world-91584.htm)

He was talking to *Fortune* about the near future of AI. His argument is blunt. By 2030, he said, two-thirds of all jobs could be performed by AI, including physicians, accountants, chip designers, and salespeople. Fifteen trillion dollars of U.S. GDP tied to labor could "mostly go away." And then he asked the question nobody in a business school wants to hear: if AI gives a farm worker the same expertise as an oncologist, do you pay them the same?

It is a provocation, yes. But it lands differently when you read it alongside [the latest layoff numbers](https://technode.global/2026/03/09/2026-tech-layoffs-reach-45000-in-march-more-than-9200-due-to-ai-and-automation-rationalfx/). As of this week, 45,000 tech workers have been cut in March 2026 alone, and over 9,200 of those layoffs have been explicitly tied to AI and automation. Companies like Block and Amazon are not being subtle about it. They are reorganizing around "more efficient, technology-driven workflows." That is corporate speak for: the machine does it now.

Meanwhile, Anthropic, the company behind the AI chatbot Claude, has built what it calls an ["early warning system"](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/anthropic-ai-jobs-most-exposed-risk/) to track which U.S. jobs are most exposed to artificial intelligence. The initial findings? Many white-collar roles sit near the front lines. But here is the nuance that got lost in the headlines: Anthropic's researchers also found "limited evidence that AI has affected employment to date." The gap between what AI *can* do and what it *is* doing remains wide. We are in the awkward middle, where the fear is outrunning the facts, but the facts are accelerating.

## The Flight to the Physical

The fear, though, is real enough to change behavior. [CNBC reports](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/03/08/jobs-apocalypse-ai-proof-skilled-trades.html) that Gen Z is increasingly turning to skilled trades, with 77% of young workers saying it is important that their future job be hard to automate. Carpentry, plumbing, electrical work. Jobs where you show up, you use your hands, and no chatbot can snake a drain.

It is a rational response. And it is not wrong, exactly. Physical presence and manual skill are genuinely harder to automate. But I think there is something more interesting happening underneath this trend. What Gen Z is really searching for is not just "AI-proof" work. They are searching for work where their contribution is visible, tangible, undeniable. Work where *they* matter, not just their output.

That instinct points toward something I have been writing about for a while now.

## The Paradox at the Center

Here is what makes this moment so disorienting. The people *building* AI are the least worried about job losses. A [survey from Technical.ly](https://technical.ly/entrepreneurship/ai-native-startups-job-growth-survey/) found that among AI-native startups, 80% expect job growth in their sector. One founder put it plainly: "AI isn't about replacing human connection, but enabling it at scale, so people can focus on sharing meaning."

Read that again. *So people can focus on sharing meaning.*

That is not a throwaway quote from a pitch deck. That is the thesis of the entire transition we are living through. The people closest to the technology see what it actually does: it removes the repetitive, the mechanical, the grindwork. What it leaves behind is the work that requires a human being. Judgment. Context. Relationship. Creativity.

Or, as I frame it in my book: Interpretation, Integration, Interpersonal Connection, and Imagination.

And then there is [this piece from Business Insider](https://www.businessinsider.com/ai-biggest-winners-farms-mines-trucks-blue-collar-software-2026-3), where the CEO of a $15 billion AI company argues that the biggest winners from AI will not be software companies at all. They will be mines, farms, and trucking operations. The physical economy. The places where AI augments human capability rather than replacing it.

## What the 4I Model Actually Predicts

When Khosla says expertise will be free, he is describing the end of one kind of value. The kind built on knowing things that other people do not know. AI collapses that advantage overnight. If a chatbot can diagnose as well as a specialist, then the diagnosis is no longer what makes the specialist valuable.

But that is only half the story. What remains scarce, what becomes *more* valuable in a world of free expertise, is the ability to do four things.

**Interpret** what the data actually means for a specific person in a specific context. AI can generate a cancer screening report. It cannot sit across from a frightened patient and help them understand what it means for their life.

**Integrate** knowledge across domains that the machine treats as separate. The best business decisions have always come from people who can hold marketing, finance, operations, and human behavior in their head at the same time. AI can analyze each silo. Connecting them is still a human act.

**Connect interpersonally** in ways that build trust, loyalty, and collaboration. The AI startup founders know this. The reason 80% of them expect more jobs is that their companies still need people who can sell, manage, mentor, negotiate, and lead. Machines do not inspire confidence. People do.

**Imagine** what does not yet exist. Not recombine existing patterns, which is what large language models do, but envision genuinely new possibilities. The trades are booming partly because they require imagination in the moment: every job site is different, every problem is novel, every solution is improvised.

## What This Means for Students (and the Rest of Us)

The labor market right now is sending contradictory signals. Layoffs are up. Hiring is up. Trades are booming. White-collar work is under pressure. AI companies are optimistic. Everyone else is nervous.

But if you look at all of these stories through one lens, they are telling the same story. The grind is ending. The repetitive, knowledge-hoarding, credential-dependent version of professional work is being automated, outsourced to machines, or restructured out of existence. What is left is the irreducibly human work.

And the question for every professional, every student, every institution is the same: are you building the skills that remain when the grind is gone?

If Khosla is right that expertise becomes free, then the premium shifts to what you do *with* expertise. How you interpret it. How you integrate it. How you connect with the people who need it. How you imagine what comes next.

That is not a crisis. That is a career strategy.
